
 CHAPTER 1
THE MEANING OF THE NAME ‘ISRAEL’
Origin of the Name ‘Israel’
There are two different traditions in Israel regarding the origin of its name. These
traditions are ancient because they have been contained in Hosea 12:4-5. According
to one account (Gen.32:22-31), the name Israel was bestowed on Jacob at phanuel
near East of the Jordan. According to the other account (Gen. 35:9-15), God changed
the patriarch’s name from Jacob to Israel after he had left phanuel and returned
to Bethel. The first account ventures a folk etymology and is boldly anthropomorphic.
It also depicts Jacob wrestling with God or the Angel of the Lord and stresses the
patriarch’s victory, whereas the second account offers no conjecture on the origin
or meaning of the name. From the view point of etymology, the actual derivation and
meaning of Israel (yistel) still remains uncertain up to date.
However, it is believed that it is theophoric, proper noun “el” (God), as in El-lohim,
El-shadah. If the verbal component is the root “Sry”, the name “Israel” means “God,
contends”. But if related root is “Srr” or yrs (both of which occur in Arabic but in
the name Hebrew, the name Israel” would mean “God shines forth” or “God heals”
“Israel” is from respectively.
Usage of the Name Israel
The name Israel implies the Biblical name of the people of God and of its anonymous
ancestor who was also called Jacob. The Bible insists that the name Israel was
conferred on the patriarch Jacob by God. Yet there are divergent accounts of its
bestowal on him and various theories regarding its meaning. Besides, being used to
designate the Patriarch, the name “Israel” is more frequently used as a collective
title for his blood or spiritual descendants, “the children of Israel” or “the House of
8
God.” The name “Israel” is also used as a mere substitute for the personal name
Jacob. It is frequently used as tribe of Israel. Thus, in Ex. 1:1 the name “Israel” is
frequent in the phrase “the sons or children of Israel (b’ne yisrael), which is used of
the immediate sons of Jacob. It is used along with such terms as “the seed of Israel,
the house of Israel, and the assembly of Israel to describe the descendants of
Jacob.
The name Israel alone is applied to the immediate family of Jacob in Gen. 34:7. Prior
to the secession of the Northern tribes and again after the restoration of the
people of the Southern kingdom, Israel designated the entire people of God. During
the period of the existence of the Northern kingdom, Israel signified that kingdom
in contradistinction to the Southern kingdom which was called Judah. In postexilic
times, Israel was occasionally used to designate the laity in contrast to the priests,
the Levitical Orders and the temple servant (I Chr. 9:2; Ezr 6:16, Neh 11:3). As used
by St. Paul, the term is more complex. It may signify the elect of the new
dispensation, the “Israel of God” (Gal 6:19), the unconverted Jews, “Israel according
to the flesh” (I Cor. 10:18).

CHAPTER 2
FORMATIVE PERIOD OF ISRAELITES’ HISTORY
The history of the Old Testament is the history of the people of Israel (also called
Hebrews or Jews). In this chapter, we will explore an overview of Old Testament
history. The history of Israel will be highlighted with the following periods: The
Patriarchal Age, The Exodus from Egypt, The Conquest of Canaan, The United
Monarchy, The Divided Kingdom, The Exilic and Post Exilic periods.
The Patriarchal Age
After the accounts of creation and the flood (Gen. 1-11), the history of Israel begins
with the call and life of Abraham. Abraham, Isaac and Jacob were the patriarchs of
Israel. The patriarchs believed God would give them (or their descendants) Canaan
as the Land of Promise (Gen. 12:1; 26:3; 18:13). While they never possessed the Land,
their dead bodies were buried in the Cave of Machpelah (Gen. 23:10-20) in Palestine.
The sons of Jacob actually became the twelve tribes of Israel (Gen. 35:22b-26). As
a result of famine they went to Egypt. There Joseph took care of them. They settled
in Goshen and they prospered greatly.
The Exodus from Egypt
Around 13th (or 14th) century B. C. the Israelites left Egypt. With their exodus
from Egypt and deliverance at the Red Sea they embarked on a journey to the
wilderness to worship the Lord under the leadership of Moses (Ex. 5:1-3). At Mount
Sinai they entered into covenant relationship with God and they became a nation (Ex.
19:1-25). Page H. Kelley (1979, 5) explained the significance of exodus event thus:
“Few events in history have had such far-reaching effects as Israel’s Exodus out of
Egypt. The Exodus event lies at the very heart of the Old Testament. The Exodus
is to the Old Covenant what the resurrection is to the New.
10
The Conquest of Canaan
While very close to the Promised Land they hesitated to enter because of a costly
unbelief actually regarded to be rebellion against the Lord. They wandered in the
wilderness and the generation passed away. It was the task of Joshua to lead them
to enter, fight, conquer and apportion the land. While in the Promised Land they
were ruled by judges, charismatic leaders- prominent among who were Deborah,
Gideon and Samson. The period is said to be a dark period for the Hebrews because
“life consisted primarily of a struggle for survival.” However Samuel was born in that
period (at 1105 B.C) and his life became significant for the establishment of Israel’s
prophetic ministry, especially the seer type.
The United Monarchy
While Samuel led the Israelites well his sons could not be trusted to succeed him.
Therefore, the people asked Samuel to give them a king especially, who could lead
them in wars (I Sam. 8:1-9). With the approval of God, Samuel anointed Saul who
however, unfortunately, did not rule in obedience to God and the Kingdom was taken
away from him. David was anointed to rule in place of Saul. He united the tribes and
defeated the Philistines (2 Sam. 5:17-25; 8:1). His dynasty was powerful and it lasted
four hundred years. The reign of Solomon, son of David (970-930 B.C) can be said
to be the golden era of Israelites history. He was wealthy and famous. He also built
the great temple.
The Divided Kingdom
During the reign of Solomon he used forced labour (1 Kings 12:1-4). When he died
the people rebelled. The Kingdom was divided into two. Rehoboam the Son of Solomon
became King of the Southern Kingdom with two tribes of Judah (922-915 B.C) and
capital in Jerusalem. Jeroboam, one of the officials of Solomon ruled the ten tribes
11
of the Northern Kingdom of Israel with capital in Samaria (922-901). The Southern
Kingdom was more stable because of its devotion to the Davidic dynasty and also
geographical location that reduced its being constantly invaded. The Northern
Kingdom was more unstable. In a period of almost 200 years there were 9 dynasties
and 19 kings, whom died violent deaths. However because of idolatry and related sins
God allowed the two kingdoms to fall (2 Kings 17:7-23). The Northern Kingdom fell
to the Assyrian invasion in 722 B.C. Samaria was captured and the people were taken
to exile by the Assyrians. In a similar manner the Southern Kingdom fell in 597 and
589 B.C to Nebuchadnezzar. Jerusalem was destroyed and the people were deported
to Babylon.
Exilic and Post Exilic Periods
During the exile some faithful worshippers of Yahweh remained in Palestine (Jer.
41:4ff). However, syncretism developed among some of those who remained and
those that the Babylonians settled in their land. The Jews in Babylon practiced a
worship that did not include Temple Sacrifices. Synagogue worships that center on
the reading of Torah most likely developed during this period. Francisco explains
(1973, 69): Such faithfulness under trying circumstances encouraged an exclusivism
that magnified the differences not only between them and the Babylonians but also
between them and those remaining in Judah… Upon their return this feeling of
superiority forced a cleavage between them and those who remained (Ezra 4:1ff)”.
This development contributed to the rise of Judaism. During the Persian Era Cyrus
permitted Jews to return to Palestine (Isa. 45:1-6). The Temple was rebuilt (538-
516 B.C). Nehemiah rebuilt protecting walls in 444 B.C. while Ezra led in spiritual
reforms.

CHAPTER 3
BIBLICAL INSPIRATION
General Meaning of Inspiration
The word inspiration comes from the Latin inspirare, meaning “to breathe into.”
Generally, it refers to a divine or supernatural influence that moves a person to act,
speak, or write beyond ordinary human ability.
In everyday use, inspiration might mean a poet being moved by beauty or an artist
stirred by emotion. However, in theology, it carries a much deeper sense—
specifically, the divine influence by which God guided the writers of Scripture so
that their writings convey His message accurately and authoritatively.
Inspiration as It Applies to the Bible
Biblical inspiration refers to the mysterious process by which God’s Spirit
superintended human authors so that the Scriptures, in their original writings, are
both fully divine and fully human.
Key Text: 2 Timothy 3:16–17 — “All Scripture is given by inspiration of God…”
(Greek: theopneustos — “God-breathed”).
Also see: 2 Peter 1:20–21 — “Holy men of God spoke as they were moved by the Holy
Spirit.”
Thus, inspiration ensures that what the Bible says, God says. It is not merely a
record of divine ideas but the very Word of God expressed through human language.
Inspiration as It Applies to the Composition of the Old Testament
In the Old Testament, inspiration worked through prophets, priests, and inspired
scribes whom God used as His spokesmen.
• The phrase “Thus says the Lord” (e.g., Isaiah 1:2; Jeremiah 1:9) reveals the
prophetic consciousness of speaking God’s very words.
13
• The Holy Spirit guided these men, using their vocabulary, culture, and
experiences, yet ensuring the message remained God’s truth.
• The Law (Torah), Prophets, and Writings were all recognized by Israel
as divinely authoritative because they originated from God’s inspiration (see
Nehemiah 9:30; Zechariah 7:12).
Therefore, the Old Testament is not just a collection of human reflections about
God but the inspired record of God’s self-disclosure to His people.
Theories of Biblical Inspiration
Different theologians and traditions have tried to explain how inspiration occurred.
Below are five (5) major theories:
1. Neo-Orthodox Theory
The neo-orthodox view of inspiration emphasizes the transcendence of God. Neoorthodoxy teaches that God is so completely different from us that the only way we
could ever know Him is through direct revelation (Trembath, 1987). This view of the
transcendence of God denies any concept of natural theology (i.e., that God can be
known through His creation).
Furthermore, neo-orthodoxy denies that the Bible is the Word of God. Rather, the
Bible is a witness, or mediator, to the Word of God, Jesus. The neo-orthodox theory
of inspiration is that the words in the Bible aren’t God’s words, but they are fallible
words written by fallible men. The Bible is only “inspired” in that God can sometimes
use the words to speak to individuals.
In summary:
• Popularized by Karl Barth and others.
• Claims that the Bible becomes the Word of God when it speaks personally to
an individual through the Holy Spirit.
14
• Scripture is a witness to divine revelation, not revelation itself.
• Strength: Emphasizes personal encounter with God.
• Weakness: Makes biblical authority subjective and variable.
2. Dictation Theory
The dictation theory of inspiration sees God as the author of Scripture and the
individual human agents as secretaries or amanuenses taking dictation. God spoke,
and the man wrote it down. This view has some merit since we know there are portions
of Scripture in which God essentially says, “Write this down” (e.g., Jeremiah 30:2),
but not all Scripture was created that way. The Pentateuch is essentially a chronicle
of the Jewish people before settling in the Promised Land.
While Moses is the primary author, much of the Pentateuch required editorial work
on Moses’ part, as he undoubtedly compiled earlier records for some of the history
(Geisler, 1968). Luke states in the preamble to his gospel that he performed detailed
research into the events of the life of Jesus before writing (Luke 1:1–4). Many of
the prophetic books read like journals of the prophets’ lives. The bottom line is that
the dictation theory only explains certain portions of Scripture, but not all of it or
even most of it.
In summary:
• Teaches that the human writers were passive instruments, writing down
exactly what God dictated word-for-word.
• The writers functioned like secretaries taking divine dictation.
• Strength: Emphasizes the Bible’s divine authority.
• Weakness: Neglects human personality, style, and historical context evident
in Scripture.
15
3. Limited or Partial Inspiration Theory
The theory of limited inspiration is the opposite view of the dictation theory.
Whereas the latter sees Scripture as primarily God’s work with minimal human
contribution, the former sees Scripture as primarily man’s work with limited help
from God. The theory of limited inspiration says that God guided the human authors
but allowed them the freedom to express themselves in their works, even to the
point of allowing factual and historical errors (Basinger & Basinger, 1983).
Fortunately, the Holy Spirit prevented doctrinal errors. The problem with this view
is that, if the Bible is prone to error in its historical accounts, then how can we trust
it in doctrinal matters? With limited inspiration, the reliability of the Bible is called
into doubt. This view also seems to ignore the fact that the Bible’s story of
redemption from Genesis to Revelation is told against the backdrop of human
history—the doctrine is woven within the history. We can’t arbitrarily say that the
history is inaccurate and then state it contains a kernel of doctrinal truth.
In summary:
• Holds that only the moral and religious teachings of the Bible are inspired,
while historical or scientific statements may contain human error.
• Strength: Addresses apparent factual inconsistencies.
• Weakness: Undermines the Bible’s authority and unity as God’s Word.
4. Dynamic or Content Theory
This theory explains that the thoughts contained in the Bible are inspired, but the
words used were left to the individual writers. This suggests the underlying message
of the scriptures is inspired, while the exact wording is dynamic. The theory
suggests, therefore, that divine inspiration only occurred at the initial moment when
16
God communicated to the authors of the scriptures; the process did not continue
through the writing of scripture.
In summary:
• Argues that the ideas or thoughts of Scripture were inspired, not necessarily
the specific words.
• God inspired the concepts, and the writers expressed them in their own
language and style.
• Strength: Acknowledges human literary freedom.
• Weakness: May weaken confidence in verbal accuracy.
5. Verbal Plenary Theory
The final view, and the view of orthodox Christianity, is the theory of plenary, verbal
inspiration. The word plenary means “complete or full,” and verbal means “the very
words of Scripture.” So plenary, verbal inspiration is the view that every single word
in the Bible is the very word of God (Abraham, 1982). It’s not just the ideas or
thoughts that are inspired, but the words themselves. Second Timothy 3:16–17 uses
a unique Greek word, theopneustos, which means “God-breathed.” Scripture is
“breathed out” of the mouth of God. The Bible’s words are God’s words.
In summary:
• Teaches that every word (verbal) and every part (plenary) of Scripture is
inspired by God.
• God guided the writers so that their words precisely expressed His intended
message without suppressing their individuality.
• This view is held by most Evangelical scholars.
• Scriptural support: Matthew 5:18; 1 Corinthians 2:13.
17
• Strength: Balances divine inspiration with human participation.
Usefulness of Inspiration Theories to Christianity
1. Affirms the Authority of Scripture:
Understanding inspiration strengthens the believer’s confidence that the
Bible is truly the Word of God (2 Timothy 3:16–17).
2. Guides Interpretation:
Theories like the Verbal Plenary model help interpreters respect both divine
authority and human context.
3. Supports Doctrine Formation:
Sound doctrine depends on recognizing Scripture as divinely inspired, not
merely human opinion (2 Peter 1:20–21).
4. Promotes Spiritual Growth:
A correct view of inspiration encourages believers to read Scripture
reverently, trusting it as God’s living Word (Hebrews 4:12).
5. Preserves Unity of the Faith:
When Christians hold to a common understanding of divine inspiration, it
fosters unity in teaching, preaching, and moral conviction.
Conclusion
Biblical inspiration is the divine act by which God communicated His truth through
human authors without error in the original manuscripts.
While theories differ on how this process occurred, the essential truth remains: the
Bible is God’s trustworthy, inspired revelation to humanity.
“The grass withers, the flower fades, but the word of our God stands forever.”
— Isaiah 40:8

CHAPTER 4
CANONIZATION
Meaning of the Term “Canon”
The word canon comes from the Greek word kanōn, meaning a rule, measuring rod, or
standard. In theological usage, it refers to the official list of books recognized as
inspired and authoritative Scripture.
Therefore, the canon of Scripture is the collection of sacred writings which the
community of faith acknowledges as divinely inspired and binding for faith and
practice.
In simple terms:
The canon defines what belongs to the Bible and what does not.
The process of canonization refers to the historical and spiritual steps by which
certain writings were recognized, preserved, and officially accepted as part of the
Holy Scriptures.
Process of the Canonization of the Old Testament
The canonization of the Old Testament was a gradual process, guided by divine
providence and the recognition of the Jewish community. It was not determined by
human decision alone but by the inherent authority of the writings themselves, which
the people of God acknowledged over time.
The process can be viewed in three main stages corresponding to the major divisions
of the Hebrew Bible:
Stage 1: The Torah (Law)
• The Torah, or Pentateuch (Genesis–Deuteronomy), was recognized first.
• It was written mainly by Moses and accepted immediately as God’s Word (see
Deuteronomy 31:24–26).
• The Law served as the foundation for Israel’s religious and national life.
19
• By around 400 B.C., the Torah was already considered canonical and read
publicly in synagogues (Nehemiah 8:1–8).
Stage 2: The Nebiim (Prophets)
• The Prophets include both Former Prophets (Joshua, Judges, Samuel, Kings)
and Latter Prophets (Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and the Twelve Minor
Prophets).
• These writings were recognized as authoritative because they were produced
by God’s messengers who spoke under divine inspiration (Zechariah 7:12).
• The prophetic books were likely accepted as a canon by about 200 B.C.
Stage 3: The Ketubiim (Writings)
• The Writings include poetic, wisdom, and historical books such as Psalms,
Proverbs, Job, Daniel, Ezra–Nehemiah, and Chronicles.
• This section took longer to gain universal acceptance because the writings
were diverse in form and use.
• Their final recognition as canonical likely occurred around 100 B.C. to 90 A.D.,
possibly confirmed at the Council of Jamnia (Jabneh) around A.D. 90.
Different Applications of “Canon” with Reference to the Old Testament
The term canon has been applied in several related senses concerning the Old
Testament:
1. Canon as a Collection:
Refers to the gathered list of sacred writings accepted as Scripture (e.g., the
24 books of the Hebrew Bible).
2. Canon as a Rule of Faith:
Denotes the standard by which all beliefs and practices are measured;
Scripture serves as the rule and guide for doctrine and conduct.
20
3. Canon as a Boundary Line:
Indicates the fixed limits distinguishing inspired books from non-inspired or
apocryphal writings.
4. Canon as an Authoritative Scripture:
Stresses the divine authority attached to the accepted books, distinguishing
them from merely historical or devotional literature.
Criteria for Old Testament Canonization
The Jewish community used certain tests or criteria—whether formally or
informally—to determine which books were genuinely inspired and canonical. These
included:
1. Prophetic Origin:
The book must be written or authorized by a recognized prophet or inspired
person (e.g., Moses, Isaiah, Jeremiah).
2. Divine Inspiration and Authority:
The content must show evidence of being God-breathed, carrying divine
power and truth (cf. Deut. 18:18–22).
3. Harmony with the Law (Torah):
No book could contradict the Law of Moses, which served as the foundation
of faith.
4. Religious and Moral Value:
The book must promote true worship, righteousness, and reverence for God.
5. Acceptance by the Covenant Community:
The Jewish community, under spiritual discernment, recognized the book’s
divine authority and used it in worship and teaching.
21
6. Historical Authenticity:
The book had to reflect accurate historical and theological continuity with
Israel’s experience of God.
Identification and Canonization of the Canonical Books
The Hebrew Bible (Tanakh) consists of three major divisions: Torah (Law), Nebiim
(Prophets), and Ketubiim (Writings) — together forming the acronym TaNaKh.
A. The Torah (Law)
• Books: Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy.
• Authorship: Traditionally ascribed to Moses.
• Recognition: Immediately regarded as divine revelation (Exodus 24:4; Deut.
31:24–26).
• Canonical Role: Foundation of all Scripture; sets the covenantal and moral
framework for Israel.
B. The Nebiim (Prophets)
• Divided into:
o Former Prophets: Joshua, Judges, Samuel, Kings.
o Latter Prophets: Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and the Twelve Minor
Prophets (Hosea–Malachi).
• Recognition: Regarded as the continuation of God’s revelation through His
chosen messengers.
• Canonical Role: Interpreted history and called Israel back to covenant
faithfulness.
C. The Ketubiim (Writings)
• Includes: Psalms, Proverbs, Job, Song of Songs, Ruth, Lamentations,
Ecclesiastes, Esther, Daniel, Ezra–Nehemiah, and Chronicles.
22
• Nature: Diverse in content—poetry, wisdom, history, and devotion.
• Recognition: Gradually accepted through liturgical use and prophetic
endorsement.
• Canonical Role: Reflects the wisdom, worship, and lived experience of God’s
people.

counts 39 books.

Usefulness of the Old Testament Canonization to Christianity
1. Affirms the Authority of Scripture:
Canonization assures believers that the Old Testament books we have are
truly inspired and trustworthy.
2. Preserves Doctrinal Purity:
By distinguishing inspired books from spurious writings, the canon guards
against false teaching.
23
3. Provides Historical Continuity:
The canon connects the Old and New Testaments, showing God’s consistent
revelation across history.
4. Guides Faith and Practice:
The recognized canon serves as a divine standard for belief, worship, and
moral conduct.
5. Strengthens Confidence in God’s Word:
Understanding the canonization process deepens faith in the Bible as a
product of divine supervision, not human selection alone.
Conclusion
The canonization of the Old Testament was both a spiritual and historical process
by which God guided His people to recognize His inspired Word. Through careful
preservation, prophetic authority, and community discernment, the Hebrew
Scriptures were recognized as the divine standard for faith and life.

CHAPTER 5
GENRES OF LITERATURE IN THE OLD TESTAMENT
Meaning of the Term “Genre”
The term “genre” comes from the French word meaning “kind” or “type.” In
literature, genre refers to a category or class of writing that shares a similar form,
style, or content.
In biblical studies, literary genre helps readers understand how a text communicates
its message. Recognizing the genre of a passage enables accurate interpretation by
showing what kind of writing it is—poetry, history, prophecy, or wisdom, for example.
In essence, identifying a genre allows readers to interpret Scripture according to
its intended meaning and literary form.
Major Genres of Literature in the Old Testament
The Old Testament contains a variety of literary genres. These reflect the richness
of God’s revelation expressed through different forms of writing suited to diverse
historical and spiritual purposes.
1. Historical Literature/Narrative
Historical literature (also known as Narrative) records events, people, and divine
acts in Israel’s history. It focuses on God’s covenantal dealings with His people
through time.
Examples:
• Genesis, Exodus, Joshua, Judges, Ruth, 1 & 2 Samuel, 1 & 2 Kings, Ezra,
Nehemiah, Esther, and Chronicles.
Purpose:
• To preserve Israel’s sacred history.
• To demonstrate God’s faithfulness and sovereignty in guiding His people.
25
• To teach moral and spiritual lessons through historical narratives.
2. Poetic Literature
Poetic literature uses figurative language, parallelism, rhythm, and imagery to
express emotions, worship, and the human experience before God.
Examples:
• Psalms, Song of Songs, and Lamentations (also parts of Job and Prophets
contain poetic sections).
Features:
• Rich imagery and symbolism.
• Use of parallelism (e.g., repeating or contrasting ideas).
• Expression of praise, lament, thanksgiving, and trust.
Purpose:
• To express devotion and prayer.
• To lead God’s people in worship.
• To communicate divine truth through artistic language.
3. Wisdom Literature
Wisdom literature offers practical and philosophical reflections on life, morality,
suffering, and the fear of God. It emphasizes living wisely in a complex world.
Examples:
• Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, and Job.
Themes:
• The fear of the Lord as the foundation of wisdom (Proverbs 1:7).
• The meaning of life and the limits of human understanding (Ecclesiastes).
26
• The problem of suffering and divine justice (Job).
Purpose:
• To provide moral instruction and life guidance.
• To teach discernment and humility before God.
• To encourage faithfulness amid life’s challenges.
4. Prophetic Literature
Prophetic literature contains messages from God delivered through His prophets to
Israel and the nations. It includes calls to repentance, warnings of judgment, and
promises of restoration.
Examples:
• Major Prophets: Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, Daniel.
• Minor Prophets: Hosea through Malachi.
Features:
• “Thus says the Lord” formula emphasizing divine authority.
• Use of visions, symbols, and allegories.
• Mixture of poetry and prose.
• Forward-looking elements predicting the coming of the Messiah and God’s
kingdom.
Purpose:
• To confront sin and call people back to covenant faithfulness.
• To reveal God’s plan for salvation and justice.
• To provide hope for future redemption.
27
Importance of Understanding Biblical Genres
1. Proper Interpretation:
Recognizing genre prevents misinterpretation—for example, reading poetry as
literal history or prophecy as mere allegory.
2. Appreciation of Diversity:
The variety of genres reveals the richness of God’s revelation and His
communication through different human experiences.
3. Balanced Spiritual Growth:
Each genre contributes uniquely—history builds faith, poetry inspires worship,
wisdom shapes character, and prophecy directs hope.
4. Enhanced Teaching and Preaching:
Understanding genre helps ministers and students communicate biblical truths
effectively and contextually.
Conclusion
The Old Testament is not a single type of book but a library of sacred writings—
each genre serving a distinct role in revealing God’s nature and purpose. Recognizing
these literary forms deepens comprehension of Scripture and enables believers to
respond to God’s Word intelligently and reverently.

CHAPTER 6
ISRAEL’S NATIONHOOD 1– GOD’S CALL OF ABRAHAM
(Genesis 12:1–7)
Introduction
The history of Israel as a nation begins with God’s call of Abraham (originally Abram)
from among the nations. This divine call marks a turning point in biblical history, the
beginning of God’s redemptive plan through a chosen people.
Before Abraham, humanity had fallen into widespread idolatry and rebellion (Genesis
11:1–9). In response, God initiated a new beginning through one man, whose
descendants would become His covenant people, the nation of Israel.
“Now the Lord said to Abram: ‘Go from your country and your kindred and your
father’s house to the land that I will show you.’” — Genesis 12:1
Meaning and Background of the Call
Abraham’s call is both a personal summons and a divine commissioning. It represents
God’s sovereign choice of a man through whom He would bless the entire world
(Genesis 12:3).
• Location: Abraham was living in Ur of the Chaldeans, a prosperous city in
Mesopotamia known for idol worship (Joshua 24:2).
• Divine Initiative: The call did not arise from Abraham’s merit or pursuit of
God but from God’s grace and purpose.
• Purpose: To establish a covenant people through whom salvation would come —
ultimately fulfilled in Jesus Christ, Abraham’s descendant (Galatians 3:16).
The Process of God’s Call of Abraham (Genesis 12:1–7)
The call of Abraham unfolded in several progressive steps, each reflecting
obedience, faith, and divine promise.
29
Step 1: The Command to Leave (Genesis 12:1)
“Leave your country, your people, and your father’s household and go to the land I
will show you.”
• God’s command involved separation — Abraham was to leave his homeland,
culture, and family ties.
• It was a call to faith: God did not reveal the destination immediately but
required Abraham to trust His guidance (Hebrews 11:8).
• The call demanded total dependence on God’s promise rather than human
security.
Step 2: The Promise of Blessing (Genesis 12:2–3)
“I will make of you a great nation, and I will bless you... and in you all the families of
the earth shall be blessed.”
God gave sevenfold promises that formed the foundation of His covenant with
Abraham:
1. I will make you a great nation.
2. I will bless you.
3. I will make your name great.
4. You will be a blessing.
5. I will bless those who bless you.
6. I will curse those who curse you.
7. In you all families of the earth shall be blessed.
These promises encompass nationhood, divine protection, and global redemption,
revealing God’s plan to reach all nations through Abraham’s lineage.
30
Step 3: Abraham’s Obedience (Genesis 12:4–5)
“So Abram departed as the Lord had spoken to him…”
• Abraham obeyed immediately and set out with Sarai (Sarah), Lot, and their
possessions toward Canaan.
• His obedience demonstrated faith in God’s word despite uncertainty (Romans
4:20–22).
• His journey symbolized a pilgrimage of faith, setting a pattern for all believers
who walk by faith, not sight.
Step 4: Arrival in Canaan and Divine Confirmation (Genesis 12:6–7)
“Then the Lord appeared to Abram and said, ‘To your offspring I will give this land.’”
• Upon reaching Shechem, Abraham received a visible manifestation of God’s
presence.
• God reaffirmed His promise — the land of Canaan would belong to Abraham’s
descendants.
• Abraham responded by building an altar, signifying worship, gratitude, and
covenant relationship.
4. Theological Significance of Abraham’s Call
1. Divine Election:
God chose Abraham by His own will and purpose (Genesis 18:19). This marks
the beginning of the biblical doctrine of election and covenant.
2. Faith and Obedience:
Abraham’s response models the nature of true faith — trust that obeys even
without full understanding (Hebrews 11:8–10).
31
3. Covenantal Relationship:
The call establishes the Abrahamic Covenant, which includes promises of land,
descendants, and blessing (Genesis 15; 17).
4. Foundation of Israel’s Nationhood:
Through Abraham’s descendants — Isaac, Jacob, and the twelve tribes — God
formed the nation of Israel (Genesis 35:10–12).
5. Universal Blessing:
God’s plan extended beyond Israel to all humanity — fulfilled in Christ, who
brings salvation to all nations (Galatians 3:8–9).
Lessons from God’s Call of Abraham
1. Obedience to God’s Word leads to blessing.
Abraham left comfort and security to follow God’s call and became the father
of faith.
2. Faith requires trust without full sight.
Abraham journeyed to an unknown land, relying solely on God’s direction.
3. God calls individuals for His redemptive purpose.
Through one man’s obedience, God began His plan of global salvation.
4. Worship and faith go hand in hand.
Abraham’s altars symbolize continuous fellowship and dependence on God.
5. Separation precedes divine blessing.
Sometimes God calls His people to leave behind old environments to fulfill His
promise.
32
Conclusion
The call of Abraham marks the birth of Israel’s nationhood and the beginning of
God’s covenant relationship with a chosen people. Abraham’s journey from Ur to
Canaan was not merely geographical—it was a spiritual pilgrimage of faith and
obedience.
Through this call, God established a model for all who would walk by faith,
demonstrating that His redemptive plan operates through divine election, covenant,
and faith-filled obedience.
“By faith Abraham obeyed when he was called to go out to a place that he was to
receive as an inheritance.” — Hebrews 11:8

CHAPTER 7
ISRAEL’S NATIONHOOD 2 – CALL OF MOSES
(Exodus 3:1–18; 20:1–20)
Introduction
The story of Moses’ call marks a major turning point in Israel’s history — the

Through Moses, God revealed Himself as Yahweh, the covenant-keeping God, and

(Exodus 19:5–6).

divine commissioning.

Moses’ Encounter with God in the Wilderness (Exodus 3:1–18)
This passage describes Moses’ call and commissioning at Mount Horeb (Sinai), where

A. The Manifestation of God (Exodus 3:1–6)

• The Burning Bush:
o God appeared as a flame of fire in a bush that burned but was not

• God’s Plan:

Lessons from Moses’ Call

The call of Moses is one of Scripture’s most profound examples of divine

Through Moses, He redeemed Israel, established His covenant law, and laid the

CHAPTER 8
MOSAIC AUTHORSHIP OF THE PENTATEUCH: AN OVERVIEW
Introduction
The Pentateuch refers to the first five books of the Bible, namely: Genesis, Exodus,
Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy.
The name Pentateuch comes from two Greek words: penta (five) and teuchos (scrolls
or volumes). These books together form the Torah (Law) in the Hebrew Bible and lay
the foundation of God’s revelation, Israel’s history, and divine law.
Traditionally, both Jewish and Christian communities have attributed the authorship
of the Pentateuch to Moses, the great leader, lawgiver, and prophet of Israel. This
traditional view is known as the Mosaic Authorship of the Pentateuch.
“Moses wrote down all the words of the Lord.” — Exodus 24:4
“Jesus said… ‘If you believed Moses, you would believe me, for he wrote of me.’” —
John 5:46
However, modern biblical scholarship has questioned this traditional belief, giving
rise to debates between arguments for and arguments against Mosaic authorship.
Proofs of Mosaic Authorship
A. Arguments for Mosaic Authorship
The case for Mosaic authorship is built on both internal evidences (within the
Pentateuch itself) and external evidences (from other parts of Scripture and
historical testimony).
40
(1) Internal Evidences for Mosaic Authorship
1. Direct Claims within the Pentateuch:
o Exodus 17:14: “Then the Lord said to Moses, ‘Write this as a memorial
in a book.’”
o Exodus 24:4: “Moses wrote all the words of the Lord.”
o Numbers 33:2: “Moses wrote their starting places, stage by stage.”
o Deuteronomy 31:9, 24: “Moses wrote this law and delivered it to the
priests.”
These passages show explicit claims that Moses wrote significant
portions of the law and narrative.
2. Eyewitness Perspective:
o The Pentateuch contains vivid, firsthand details (e.g., geographical
descriptions, personal dialogues, and exact figures) consistent with an
eyewitness account.
o For instance, the wilderness journeys, encounters with Pharaoh, and
experiences at Sinai display knowledge that suggests direct
participation.
3. Unity of Purpose and Style:
o Despite its length, the Pentateuch maintains a consistent theological
focus on covenant, law, and divine guidance — implying single or unified
authorship under Moses’ leadership.
4. Historical Continuity:
o The Pentateuch’s narrative connects naturally from the patriarchal
stories to the Exodus and the covenant at Sinai, consistent with a
historical account from one era.
41
(2) External Evidences for Mosaic Authorship
1. Old Testament Testimony:
o Joshua 8:31–32: Joshua obeyed “as Moses the servant of the Lord
commanded.”
o 1 Kings 2:3; 2 Kings 14:6: References to “the Law of Moses.”
o Ezra 6:18; Nehemiah 13:1: The law is explicitly attributed to Moses.
o The prophets also quote “the Law of Moses” (e.g., Daniel 9:11–13;
Malachi 4:4).
2. New Testament Affirmation:
o Jesus and the apostles consistently referred to Moses as the author
of the Law.
▪ Mark 12:26: “Have you not read in the book of Moses…?”
▪ Luke 24:27: Jesus explained things “in Moses and all the
Prophets.”
▪ John 5:46–47: “If you believed Moses, you would believe me, for
he wrote of me.”
o Paul also referenced “the law of Moses” (Romans 10:5).
3. Jewish Tradition:
o Ancient Jewish writings such as the Talmud and Josephus’ Antiquities
strongly uphold Mosaic authorship.
o The Samaritans and Dead Sea Scrolls communities also preserved the
Pentateuch with Mosaic attribution.
4. Early Christian Fathers:
o Early Church Fathers (e.g., Irenaeus, Origen, Augustine) affirmed
Moses as the human author under divine inspiration.
42
Arguments Against Mosaic Authorship
Despite strong traditional support, modern biblical scholarship — particularly since
the 18th and 19th centuries — has raised objections to Mosaic authorship, proposing
alternative explanations such as the Documentary Hypothesis.
These objections are also based on internal and external evidences.
(1) Internal Evidences Against Mosaic Authorship
1. Anachronisms (Chronological Inconsistencies):
o Certain phrases and references appear to reflect a later period than
Moses.
▪ Genesis 36:31: Mentions “kings who reigned in Edom before any
king reigned over the Israelites” — implying knowledge of the
monarchy, centuries after Moses.
▪ Deuteronomy 34:5–10: Describes Moses’ death and burial, which
he could not have written himself (though possibly added later
by Joshua or a scribe).
2. Use of the Phrase “Until This Day”:
o Passages such as Deuteronomy 3:14; Genesis 26:33 suggest a time lapse
between the events described and their recording.
3. Stylistic and Linguistic Differences:
o Critics observe variations in style, vocabulary, and divine names
(Yahweh and Elohim), suggesting multiple sources.
4. Double Narratives and Repetitions:
o Stories such as the creation accounts (Genesis 1 and 2) and the flood
narratives appear to have parallel versions with different emphases,
interpreted by critics as evidence of combined sources.
43
(2) External Evidences Against Mosaic Authorship
1. Documentary Hypothesis (JEDP Theory):
o Proposed by Julius Wellhausen and others, this theory suggests the
Pentateuch was composed from four main sources:
▪ J (Yahwist): Uses “Yahweh” for God, emphasizes southern
(Judah) traditions.
▪ E (Elohist): Uses “Elohim,” linked with northern (Israel)
traditions.
▪ D (Deuteronomist): Emphasizes law and moral reform (mainly
Deuteronomy).
▪ P (Priestly): Focuses on rituals, genealogy, and priestly
regulations.
o These sources were allegedly compiled by editors over centuries, long
after Moses.
2. Historical Development of Hebrew Language:
o Linguistic studies suggest some Hebrew used in the Pentateuch reflects
later stages of the language, possibly post-Mosaic.
3. Archaeological and Cultural References:
o Certain social or religious customs described in the Pentateuch appear
to reflect post-Mosaic settings, such as temple worship rather than
tabernacle service.
4. Later Editorial Additions:
o Some scholars believe later scribes (such as Ezra) may have edited,
updated, or expanded the text to fit their historical context.
44
Scholarly and Theological Reflections
1. Possible Explanation of Additions:
o Even if later editorial work occurred (e.g., the account of Moses’ death),
this does not invalidate Mosaic authorship of the core material.
o It was common in ancient literature for later inspired editors to update
place names or add explanatory notes.
2. Balanced View:
o Many conservative scholars hold that Moses was the primary author,
but the Pentateuch may include minor additions by later inspired figures
(e.g., Joshua, Ezra).
o Thus, Mosaic authorship remains the foundational view regarding
content and origin.
3. Theological Importance:
o Belief in Mosaic authorship upholds the Pentateuch’s divine unity,
historical reliability, and authority as the Word of God.
Conclusion
The debate over Mosaic authorship reflects the tension between traditional belief
and modern critical scholarship.
While internal and external evidences have been raised against Mosaic authorship,
the weight of biblical testimony, historical tradition, and theological coherence
strongly supports the view that Moses was the principal author of the Pentateuch
under divine inspiration.
“The Law was given through Moses; grace and truth came through Jesus Christ.” —
John 1:17
Moses remains central in God’s revelation as the lawgiver, prophet, and mediator of
the Old Covenant — a foreshadowing of Christ, the mediator of the New Covenant.
45
CHAPTER 9
THE DOCUMENTARY HYPOTHESIS
Introduction
The Documentary Hypothesis is one of the most influential theories in modern
biblical scholarship that attempts to explain the composition of the Pentateuch —
the first five books of the Old Testament (Genesis to Deuteronomy).
While traditional belief attributes the Pentateuch to Moses, the Documentary
Hypothesis argues that it is a composite work, drawn from multiple written sources
compiled over time by different authors or editors.
The hypothesis suggests that the Pentateuch as we have it today was formed
through the collection, editing, and combination of various documents, each
reflecting distinct historical settings, theological emphases, and writing styles.
The most common and developed form of this theory identifies four main sources:
• J – Yahwist
• E – Elohist
• D – Deuteronomist
• P – Priestly
Nature and Types of the Documentary Hypothesis
Over time, several versions of the Documentary Hypothesis emerged as scholars
refined their understanding of how the Pentateuch developed. The main forms
include:
A. The Early Documentary Hypothesis
• This theory originated in the 17th and 18th centuries as scholars noticed
different divine names (Yahweh and Elohim) in Genesis and assumed they
represented different sources.
46
• Early scholars like Jean Astruc (1753) proposed that Moses may have used
two main written documents—one using Yahweh (Jehovah) and the other using
Elohim for God.
• These were later combined into a single narrative.
• This became known as the Two-Document Theory, which later developed into
the Four-Document Theory (J, E, D, P).
B. The Fragmentary Hypothesis
• Proposed by scholars such as Alexander Geddes and Johann Vater, this view
argued that the Pentateuch did not originate from large, coherent documents
(like J or E), but from many smaller fragments — independent traditions,
stories, songs, or laws passed down orally and later compiled by editors.
• According to this theory, no major continuous documents existed; rather, the
Pentateuch was a collection of separate fragments stitched together over
centuries.
• The emphasis here is on oral tradition and gradual accumulation.
C. The Supplementary Hypothesis
• This theory proposed that there was originally a single basic narrative
document (possibly the “Elohistic” source), and other materials were later
added as supplements or expansions.
• The Deuteronomist and Priestly sources were considered later additions
intended to clarify or expand theological points.
• Scholars such as Ewald and Riehm developed this view in the 19th century.
• The Pentateuch, therefore, grew progressively, with successive inspired
editors expanding earlier texts to meet new religious needs.
47
The Classical J.E.D.P. Hypothesis
By the late 19th century, Julius Wellhausen (1844–1918) refined these earlier ideas
into what became known as the Documentary Hypothesis or J.E.D.P. Theory — the
most widely known form today.
According to Wellhausen’s model, four distinct documents (or traditions) were
combined by editors into the Pentateuch in the following approximate order:

Yahwist
(Jehovist)

48
Characteristics of the J.E.D.P. Documents
Each document displays unique linguistic, theological, and stylistic features.
Below are their key characteristics and emphases:
A. The J (Yahwist) Source
1. Name of God: Uses Yahweh (Jehovah) consistently from the beginning.
2. Geographical Focus: Southern kingdom (Judah).
3. Date: Around the 9th century BC.
4. Style: Vivid, earthy, and narrative-driven. God is described in human-like
(anthropomorphic) terms — walking, speaking, and interacting personally.
5. Theology: Emphasizes God’s closeness to humanity, covenantal promises, and
moral lessons from human relationships.
6. Key Passages: Genesis 2–3 (creation and fall), parts of Exodus and Numbers.
B. The E (Elohist) Source
1. Name of God: Uses Elohim (God) until the revelation of God’s name to Moses
(Exodus 3).
2. Geographical Focus: Northern kingdom (Israel).
3. Date: Around the 8th century BC.
4. Style: More abstract and reserved than J; emphasizes prophecy, dreams, and
moral obedience.
5. Theology: Focus on God’s transcendence and the fear of God. Mediators
(angels or prophets) often convey God’s messages.
6. Key Passages: Genesis 20–22, parts of Exodus 19–20.
49
C. The D (Deuteronomist) Source
1. Name of God: Yahweh (the LORD).
2. Geographical Focus: Jerusalem (Judah).
3. Date: 7th century BC, associated with King Josiah’s reform (2 Kings 22–23).
4. Style: Preaching, exhortative, and moralistic; uses long speeches and appeals
to obedience.
5. Theology: Emphasizes covenant relationship, law, and centralization of worship
in Jerusalem.
6. Key Passages: The Book of Deuteronomy.
D. The P (Priestly) Source
1. Name of God: Elohim until Exodus 6:2–3, when God reveals Himself as Yahweh.
2. Date: 6th century BC (Babylonian Exile).
3. Focus: Rituals, genealogies, laws, sacrifices, festivals, and priestly duties.
4. Style: Formal, repetitive, structured; emphasizes order and holiness.
5. Theology: Highlights God’s transcendence, holiness, and covenant through
rituals and institutions.
6. Key Passages: Genesis 1 (creation), Leviticus, and much of Numbers.
Critical Evaluation of the Documentary Hypothesis
A. Strengths
• Provides a literary explanation for differences in style, repetition, and divine
names.
• Encourages deeper study of historical and theological development in Israel’s
religion.
50
• Recognizes the role of editors and oral tradition in the Bible’s transmission.
B. Weaknesses
• Overemphasis on stylistic differences that could reflect literary variety
rather than multiple authors.
• Lacks solid manuscript evidence of the supposed J, E, D, or P sources.
• Undermines biblical unity and divine inspiration if taken rigidly.
• Conservative scholars argue that these differences are purposeful theological
emphases within a single divinely guided composition.
Conclusion
The Documentary Hypothesis remains a major academic framework for studying the
Pentateuch’s formation.
While it offers valuable insights into literary analysis, it does not necessarily negate
Mosaic involvement or divine inspiration.
Many scholars today take a moderate position, acknowledging possible sources and
redactions while affirming the theological unity and divine origin of the Pentateuch.
“All Scripture is given by inspiration of God…” — 2 Timothy 3:16
The study of the Documentary Hypothesis thus helps students appreciate both the
human and divine dimensions of Scripture — its historical depth, literary richness,
and enduring revelation.
51
CHAPTER 10
THE RISE OF MONARCHY IN ISRAEL
(Historical Setting for the Establishment of the Monarchy – 1 Samuel 1–7)
Introduction
The rise of monarchy in Israel did not happen suddenly; it emerged from a long
process of social, religious, and political transformation that took place during the
final years of the period of the Judges.
The first seven chapters of 1 Samuel (1 Samuel 1–7) provide the historical
background that explains why Israel desired a king.
These chapters cover the decline of priestly leadership under Eli, the birth and
ministry of Samuel, and the national crises that exposed Israel’s need for
centralized leadership.
Thus, the monarchy arose as a response to moral decay, religious corruption, political
instability, and military weakness — all of which created the conditions for a new
form of governance.
Historical Background (1 Samuel 1–7)
The events of 1 Samuel 1–7 fall between the end of the Judges period and the
beginning of the kingship era. This period was characterized by spiritual decline and
national disorganization.
1. Religious Corruption under Eli (1 Samuel 1–3):
o The priesthood, which should have preserved Israel’s spiritual life, had
become corrupt.
o Eli’s sons, Hophni and Phinehas, abused their priestly office by
extorting offerings and engaging in immorality at Shiloh (1 Samuel 2:12–
17, 22).
52
o The moral failure of the priests led to a general loss of respect for
divine worship.
2. Loss of National Identity (1 Samuel 4):
o Israel treated the Ark of the Covenant superstitiously, carrying it into
battle against the Philistines as a magical charm.
o God allowed the Ark to be captured, symbolizing the departure of His
glory (Ichabod – 1 Samuel 4:21).
o The defeat by the Philistines marked a deep national humiliation and
spiritual crisis.
3. Divine Judgment and the End of Eli’s Era:
o The death of Eli and his sons (1 Samuel 4:17–18) fulfilled the prophetic
judgment announced earlier by the man of God and by Samuel (1 Samuel
2:27–36; 3:11–14).
o This signaled the end of hereditary priestly leadership as the main form
of authority in Israel.
4. Rise of Samuel (1 Samuel 3–7):
o God called Samuel as a prophet and judge to restore divine order.
o Under Samuel’s ministry, the people repented, and the Ark was
restored to Israel (1 Samuel 7:2–4).
o Samuel led Israel to victory against the Philistines at Mizpah, marking
the beginning of national renewal.
o However, the system of judges remained temporary and inadequate to
ensure lasting stability.
53
3. Factors that Gave Rise to the Monarchy
The demand for a king later in 1 Samuel 8 can only be understood in light of the
developments in 1 Samuel 1–7.
Several interrelated factors contributed to the establishment of monarchy in
Israel:
A. Religious Factors
1. Corruption in the Priesthood:
o Eli’s sons discredited the priestly office through greed and immorality
(1 Samuel 2:12–17).
o This undermined public trust in religious leadership and created a
vacuum of moral authority.
2. Loss of Spiritual Direction:
o The Ark’s capture symbolized the spiritual emptiness of Israel’s faith.
o Without godly leadership, Israel turned to human solutions, desiring a
visible king.
3. Desire for Visible Leadership:
o The people began to equate divine absence with political weakness,
seeking a king to embody God’s leadership in tangible form.
B. Political Factors
1. Tribal Disunity:
o Israel’s tribal system lacked centralized governance.
o Each tribe acted independently, weakening national solidarity (Judges
21:25).
54
2. Failure of the Judges System:
o Leadership under the judges was sporadic and localized, dependent on
charismatic figures rather than a lasting structure.
o Samuel’s aging and the corruption of his sons (1 Samuel 8:1–3) revived
old fears of disorder.
3. Need for National Organization:
o Israel’s scattered leadership made administration, defense, and justice
inconsistent.
o A monarchy promised stable governance and continuity.
C. Military Factors
1. Threat from the Philistines:
o The Philistines were Israel’s strongest military opponents during
Samuel’s time.
o Their advanced weapons and organized army constantly endangered
Israel’s survival.
o Israel desired a king “to go out before us and fight our battles” (1
Samuel 8:20).
2. Inadequate Defense System:
o The tribes lacked unified military command.
o A centralized monarchy appeared essential for defense and strategic
coordination.
D. Social and Cultural Factors
1. Influence of Neighboring Nations:
o Neighboring peoples (Moabites, Ammonites, Edomites, Philistines) all
had kings.
55
o Israel’s elders wanted to adopt similar governance to gain prestige and
political equality.
2. Desire for Progress and Civilization:
o The Israelites associated monarchy with order, sophistication, and
power.
o Their request reflected a desire to be “modern” like their neighbors,
but it also revealed spiritual compromise.
The Role of Eli in the Institution of Monarchy
Eli, the priest and judge, played a critical transitional role in Israel’s history leading
to the monarchy. His leadership highlights the decline of priestly authority and the
need for prophetic leadership.
1. Religious Decline under Eli:
o Eli’s failure to discipline his sons (1 Samuel 2:22–25) led to corruption
in the sanctuary.
o God’s judgment upon Eli’s house marked the end of priestly dominance
as the center of authority in Israel.
2. Prophetic Transition:
o Through Eli, God introduced Samuel to divine service.
o Eli recognized Samuel’s call (1 Samuel 3:8–9) and guided him spiritually.
o Thus, Eli’s era closed the period of priestly leadership and opened the
prophetic age.
3. Symbol of Institutional Weakness:
o Eli’s physical blindness and moral weakness symbolized the spiritual
blindness of Israel’s leadership.
56
o His death marked the collapse of old religious order, paving the way for
a new form of governance that would eventually become monarchy.
5. The Role of Samuel in the Institution of Monarchy
Samuel stands as the bridge between the period of the Judges and the beginning of
the Monarchy.
He combined the roles of prophet, priest, and judge, serving as God’s instrument to
guide Israel through transition.
1. Spiritual Reformation:
o Samuel called Israel to repentance and restored true worship (1 Samuel
7:3–6).
o His intercession and leadership brought peace and divine favor.
2. Political and Judicial Leadership:
o Samuel served as a judge, traveling annually between Bethel, Gilgal,
Mizpah, and Ramah (1 Samuel 7:15–17).
o His leadership united the tribes temporarily and prepared them for
centralized governance.
3. Prophetic Guidance in Establishing Monarchy:
o When the people demanded a king (1 Samuel 8), Samuel consulted God.
o Though personally displeased, he obeyed God’s command to anoint Saul
(1 Samuel 9–10).
o Thus, Samuel mediated between divine will and human desire, ensuring
that the monarchy began under prophetic supervision.
4. Guardian of Covenant Principles:
o Samuel warned the nation and its new king to remain faithful to God (1
Samuel 12).
57
o He upheld the principle that the king is subject to God’s law — a core
foundation of Israel’s monarchy.
Conclusion
The historical setting for the establishment of the monarchy (1 Samuel 1–7) reveals
that Israel’s demand for a king arose from deep-rooted crises — moral corruption,
political instability, and military weakness.
Under Eli, Israel witnessed the collapse of priestly authority; under Samuel, a new
prophetic and national leadership emerged.
Samuel’s obedience and mediation allowed God’s purpose to unfold — transforming
Israel’s desire for a human king into a divine plan that would eventually culminate in
the Davidic covenant and the reign of the Messiah, Jesus Christ, the eternal King.
“The Lord will judge His people; the Lord will rule over them.” — 1 Samuel 2:10

CHAPTER 11
THE ESTABLISHMENT OF THE MONARCHY
(1 SAMUEL 8–12)
Introduction
The establishment of the monarchy in Israel marked a turning point in the nation’s
history — from a theocratic confederation of tribes governed by judges to a unified
kingdom ruled by a human king.
1 Samuel 8–12 records this transformation under the ministry of Samuel, who served
as the last judge and the first prophetic leader to inaugurate the kingship.
This section highlights the ministry of Eli, the birth and call of Samuel, Israel’s war
with the Philistines and the capture of the Ark, and Samuel’s multifaceted role as
priest, judge, and prophet before the monarchy began.
The Ministry of Eli and the Birth of Samuel (1 Samuel 1–2)
A. The Background of Eli’s Ministry
• Eli was both a priest and judge in Israel, ministering at Shiloh, where the Ark
of the Covenant resided (1 Samuel 1:9).
• His leadership period was marked by religious corruption and moral laxity,
especially through his sons, Hophni and Phinehas, who dishonored God by
abusing their priestly office (1 Samuel 2:12–17, 22–25).
• Despite his personal reverence, Eli’s weakness in disciplining his sons led to
the spiritual decline of Israel’s worship system.
B. The Birth of Samuel
• Amid Israel’s religious decay, Hannah’s prayer for a child and her vow to
dedicate him to God (1 Samuel 1:10–11) set the stage for divine intervention.
• God answered her prayer, and Samuel was born — his name meaning “Asked of
God” (1 Samuel 1:20).
59
• Hannah fulfilled her vow, bringing Samuel to serve under Eli in the tabernacle
(1 Samuel 1:27–28).
• Samuel’s birth signified the beginning of a new era of divine communication in
Israel — the restoration of prophetic leadership.
The Call of Samuel (1 Samuel 3:1–21)
A. The Silence of God
• During Eli’s time, “the word of the Lord was rare” (1 Samuel 3:1).
• This spiritual silence reflected Israel’s alienation from God due to sin and
disobedience.
B. Samuel’s Call Experience
• While serving in the temple as a boy, Samuel heard God’s voice calling him by
name (1 Samuel 3:4–10).
• At first, he mistook the voice for Eli’s, but with Eli’s guidance, he responded,
“Speak, Lord, for your servant hears.”
• God revealed to Samuel the coming judgment upon Eli’s house for his failure
to correct his sons (1 Samuel 3:11–14).
C. The Result of the Call
• Samuel became known throughout Israel as a true prophet of the Lord (1
Samuel 3:19–21).
• His prophetic ministry marked the renewal of divine revelation in Israel and
prepared the nation for a new phase of leadership under God’s direction.
60
The War with the Philistines and the Capture of the Ark of God (1
Samuel 4:1–22)
A. Israel’s Presumptuous Battle
• The Israelites went to war against the Philistines without divine instruction
and suffered defeat (1 Samuel 4:2).
• They then brought the Ark of the Covenant from Shiloh, thinking it would
guarantee victory (1 Samuel 4:3–4).
• This act revealed Israel’s superstitious attitude toward the Ark, treating it
as a magical charm rather than a symbol of God’s presence.
B. The Defeat and the Capture of the Ark
• The Philistines defeated Israel again, killing 30,000 soldiers and capturing the
Ark (1 Samuel 4:10–11).
• Eli’s sons, Hophni and Phinehas, died in the battle, fulfilling God’s earlier
prophecy of judgment.
• When Eli heard the news, he fell backward and died (1 Samuel 4:18).
C. The Meaning of “Ichabod”
• Phinehas’s wife named her newborn son Ichabod, meaning “The glory has
departed from Israel” (1 Samuel 4:21).
• The loss of the Ark symbolized the departure of God’s presence from Israel
due to sin and disobedience.
Key Lesson: Religious symbols have no power apart from obedience and holiness. God’s
presence cannot be manipulated by rituals.
61
The Role of Samuel as Priest, Judge, and Prophet Prior to the
Monarchy
Samuel emerged as one of the most significant transitional figures in Israel’s
history, embodying three offices — priest, judge, and prophet — each essential in
preparing Israel for the monarchy.
A. Samuel as Priest
• Though not a descendant of Aaron, Samuel functioned as a Levitical priest
dedicated to God from childhood (1 Samuel 1:28; 2:11).
• He performed sacrificial duties (1 Samuel 7:9–10) and interceded for the
nation during times of crisis.
• His ministry restored true worship and holiness among the people, turning
their hearts back to God (1 Samuel 7:3–4).
B. Samuel as Judge
• Samuel succeeded Eli as a national judge, administering justice and leading the
people according to God’s law (1 Samuel 7:15–17).
• He traveled in a circuit between Bethel, Gilgal, Mizpah, and Ramah to govern
Israel fairly.
• Under his leadership, Israel enjoyed peace and stability (1 Samuel 7:13).
[
C. Samuel as Prophet
• Samuel was the first major prophet after Moses to speak God’s word to the
entire nation (1 Samuel 3:20).
• He guided Israel’s political and spiritual life by divine revelation.
• He anointed Saul as the first king (1 Samuel 10:1) and later David (1 Samuel
16:13), proving that kingship must operate under prophetic authority.
62
• Samuel’s role established the principle of theocratic monarchy — where the
king rules under God’s law.
The Establishment of the Monarchy (1 Samuel 8–12)
The culmination of Samuel’s leadership came when the people demanded a king.
A. Israel’s Request for a King (1 Samuel 8:1–9)
• As Samuel grew old, he appointed his sons as judges, but they were corrupt
and unjust (1 Samuel 8:3).
• The elders of Israel requested a king “to judge us like all the nations” (1
Samuel 8:5).
• Their demand revealed a loss of trust in divine leadership and a desire for
political conformity.
B. God’s Response
• God told Samuel, “They have not rejected you, but they have rejected Me,
that I should not reign over them” (1 Samuel 8:7).
• Though displeased, God permitted their request to fulfill His broader plan for
Israel’s future.
C. The Anointing and Acceptance of Saul (1 Samuel 9–10)
• God directed Samuel to anoint Saul, a Benjamite, as the first king of Israel (1
Samuel 10:1).
• Saul was publicly presented to the people at Mizpah (1 Samuel 10:24), and the
people shouted, “Long live the king!”
D. Samuel’s Farewell and Covenant Renewal (1 Samuel 12)
• In his farewell speech, Samuel reminded the nation of God’s faithfulness and
warned both king and people to obey God’s commands.
• He declared that obedience — not monarchy — would determine Israel’s
success (1 Samuel 12:14–15).
63
Summary
The establishment of the monarchy (1 Samuel 8–12) was the outcome of Israel’s
historical, spiritual, and political evolution:
• Eli’s failure marked the end of corrupt priestly leadership.
• Samuel’s birth and call restored divine revelation and national renewal.
• Israel’s military defeats exposed the need for unity and strong leadership.
• Samuel’s ministry as priest, judge, and prophet prepared the nation for
monarchy under divine guidance.
Though Israel’s demand for a king reflected human weakness, God transformed it
into a step toward His redemptive plan, ultimately fulfilled in Jesus Christ, the
perfect King of kings.
Factors that Led to the Request for a King
The people’s demand for monarchy was not a single event but the outcome of multiple
interconnected causes.
A. Religious Factors
1. Corruption in the Priesthood and Leadership
o Earlier, the sons of Eli had defiled the priesthood through greed and
immorality (1 Samuel 2:12–17).
o Later, Samuel’s sons also took bribes and perverted justice (1 Samuel
8:3).
o This widespread corruption created distrust in spiritual leadership,
prompting the people to seek a more organized and visible system of
governance.
64
2. Weak Spiritual Commitment
o The people’s faith in God’s invisible rule weakened.
o Instead of renewing their covenant loyalty, they looked for a human
solution to spiritual problems.
o Their request reflected a rejection of divine kingship (1 Samuel 8:7).
3. Desire for Visible Representation
o The Israelites wanted a tangible, human figure to lead them, especially
in warfare and national affairs.
o This desire echoed their ancestors’ yearning for visible signs (cf.
Exodus 32:1 – the golden calf).
B. Political Factors
1. Disunity among the Tribes
o Israel functioned as a tribal confederation with no central government.
o Each tribe handled its own affairs, leading to rivalry and lack of national
coordination.
o The people believed that a king would unify the tribes and create
national order.
2. Failure of the Judges System
o The judges were temporary leaders, raised by God for specific crises.
o This irregular and localized leadership created periods of anarchy and
instability (Judges 21:25 – “Everyone did what was right in his own
eyes”).
o A monarchy seemed to offer continuity and structure in leadership.
65
3. Need for Centralized Authority
o The people wanted a permanent ruler to oversee administration,
taxation, justice, and defense.
o They believed a king could strengthen national identity and foster
political maturity.
C. Military Factors
1. Constant Threat from the Philistines
o The Philistines were Israel’s most formidable enemies during Samuel’s
time (1 Samuel 4–7).
o Their superior military organization and iron weapons gave them
advantage over Israel’s scattered militias.
o Israel desired a king “to go out before us and fight our battles” (1
Samuel 8:20).
2. Need for Military Organization
o Without a central commander, Israel’s tribal armies lacked
coordination.
o A monarchy promised a standing army, stronger defense, and better
preparedness against foreign threats.
D. Social and Cultural Factors
1. Influence of Neighboring Nations
o Israel’s neighboring nations — the Moabites, Ammonites, Edomites, and
Philistines — all had kings.
o The Israelites envied their apparent success and wanted to be “like all
the nations” (1 Samuel 8:5).
66
o This desire reflected conformity to worldly standards rather than
confidence in God’s distinct plan.
2. Desire for National Prestige
o A monarchy was seen as a sign of civilization, strength, and prestige in
the ancient Near East.
o Israel wanted to project a similar image among other nations.
3. Weak National Identity
o The people sought unity through political means instead of spiritual
covenant renewal.
o A king was expected to serve as a symbol of national pride and unity.
E. Theological Factor – Rejection of God’s Kingship
• God Himself interpreted their demand as a rejection of His divine rule:
“They have not rejected you, but they have rejected Me, that I should not reign
over them.” — 1 Samuel 8:7
• Though God granted their request, He made clear that their desire was rooted
in spiritual rebellion, not just political necessity.
• Yet, in His sovereignty, God used this human demand to advance His
redemptive plan, ultimately leading to the Davidic dynasty and the kingship of
Christ.
God’s Warning through Samuel (1 Samuel 8:10–18)
Before granting their request, Samuel warned the people about the implications of
monarchy:
• A king would conscript their sons and daughters for war and labor.
• He would take their best lands, vineyards, and livestock.
67
• The people would become subjects and face taxation.
Despite these warnings, the people insisted:
“No, we will have a king over us!” (1 Samuel 8:19).
Their persistence demonstrated spiritual stubbornness and lack of trust in God’s
leadership.

CHAPTER 12
SAUL’S REIGN AND FAILURES
(1 SAMUEL 13–15)
Introduction
Saul, the first king of Israel, was chosen by God through the prophet Samuel in
response to the people’s demand for a human ruler. His reign marked a new era in
Israel’s political and religious history.
Saul’s kingship began with great promise — humility, divine anointing, and initial
military success — but it soon degenerated into disobedience, pride, and rejection
by God.
The account of 1 Samuel 13–15 reveals both the strengths and weaknesses of Saul’s
leadership, providing valuable lessons on the relationship between divine authority
and human responsibility.
Background to Saul’s Reign
• Saul was from the tribe of Benjamin, the son of Kish, and was described as
handsome and taller than any of the people (1 Samuel 9:1–2).
• His rise to the throne was orchestrated by God’s direct choice, communicated
through Samuel (1 Samuel 9:15–17; 10:1).
• Initially, Saul showed humility and reluctance to rule (1 Samuel 10:21–22).
• His early reign was marked by divine favor and military victories, especially
against the Ammonites (1 Samuel 11:1–11).
• However, over time, spiritual pride and disobedience led to his downfall.
69
Successes of the Reign of King Saul
Despite his later failures, Saul’s early reign recorded significant achievements that
unified and strengthened Israel.
A. Political Successes
1. Unification of the Tribes
o Before Saul, Israel was a confederation of tribes often acting
independently.
o Saul succeeded in uniting the tribes under a centralized monarchy.
o His leadership created a sense of national identity and political cohesion
(1 Samuel 11:7–15).
2. Foundation of the Monarchy
o Saul established the first national government in Israel’s history.
o He laid the groundwork for future kings by introducing administrative
and military structures.
B. Military Successes
1. Victory over the Ammonites (1 Samuel 11)
o When Nahash the Ammonite besieged Jabesh-gilead, Saul rallied the
people and won a decisive victory.
o His success brought national unity and confirmed his legitimacy as king.
o The people renewed the kingship at Gilgal in celebration of God’s
deliverance.
2. Defeat of the Philistines (1 Samuel 13–14)
o Saul and his son Jonathan fought courageously against the Philistines,
delivering Israel from oppression.
70
o Jonathan’s faith and bravery at Michmash (1 Samuel 14:1–15) inspired
Israel’s army to victory.
o Saul’s army gained control of strategic territories and strengthened
national defense.
3. Campaigns against Neighboring Nations (1 Samuel 14:47–48)
o Saul achieved victories over Moab, Ammon, Edom, the kings of Zobah,
and the Amalekites.
o His reign brought temporary peace and security to Israel’s borders.
C. Religious and Moral Successes
1. Dependence on Prophetic Guidance (Initially)
o In the beginning, Saul respected Samuel’s prophetic authority.
o He sought Samuel’s counsel before going into battle (1 Samuel 9:6–10).
o His early submission to divine instruction brought God’s blessing.
2. Promotion of National Worship
o Saul led the people in thanksgiving after military victories.
o The early phase of his reign strengthened religious observance and
national unity under God.
Summary of Saul’s Successes
• United Israel under one ruler.
• Defended the nation against its enemies.
• Established the foundation of monarchy.
• Strengthened military organization.
• Initially sought divine guidance through the prophet Samuel.
71
Factors that Caused the Failure of King Saul
Although Saul began well, his reign ended in rejection and divine displeasure.
His downfall was not due to lack of ability, but failure of character and spiritual
disobedience.
The following are key factors that contributed to his failure:
A. Impatience and Disobedience (1 Samuel 13:5–14)
• During the war against the Philistines at Gilgal, Samuel had instructed Saul to
wait seven days for him to come and offer sacrifices.
• When Samuel delayed, Saul took it upon himself to offer the burnt offering.
• His action violated priestly duties and demonstrated impatience and lack of
faith in God’s timing.
• As a result, Samuel declared that Saul’s kingdom would not continue — God
had chosen another man after His own heart (1 Samuel 13:13–14).
B. Rash Vows and Poor Judgment (1 Samuel 14:24–45)
• Saul placed his troops under an unwise oath during battle, forbidding them
from eating until evening.
• This weakened the soldiers and nearly cost Jonathan his life when he
unknowingly broke the vow.
• Saul’s impulsiveness and lack of discernment exposed his poor decision-making
as a leader.
C. Partial Obedience and Pride (1 Samuel 15:1–23)
• God commanded Saul through Samuel to utterly destroy the Amalekites and
all their possessions.
• Saul spared King Agag and the best livestock, claiming they were for sacrifice.
72
• His disobedience revealed self-interest and pride disguised as religious
devotion.
• Samuel confronted him:
“To obey is better than sacrifice, and to hearken than the fat of rams.” — 1 Samuel
15:22
• Saul’s excuses and refusal to take responsibility demonstrated spiritual
arrogance.
• Consequently, God rejected Saul from being king (1 Samuel 15:26–28).
D. Spiritual Decline and Rebellion
• As Saul drifted from obedience, the Spirit of the Lord departed from him (1
Samuel 16:14).
• He became tormented by jealousy, fear, and anger — especially toward David
(1 Samuel 18:8–12).
• Saul’s dependence on his own strength, rather than God’s guidance, led to
spiritual ruin.
E. Rejection by God
• Samuel’s pronouncement of divine rejection (1 Samuel 15:26–28) symbolized
the end of Saul’s divine favor.
• Although Saul continued to reign outwardly, he was spiritually dethroned.
• God began preparing David, a man after His own heart, to replace him (1
Samuel 16:1).
73
Theological and Moral Lessons from Saul’s Reign
1. Obedience is the foundation of true success.
o Saul’s downfall illustrates that God values obedience more than outward
achievement.
2. Pride leads to downfall.
o Saul’s pride in his achievements blinded him to his spiritual decay.
3. God’s anointing is not a guarantee of lasting success.
o Continued obedience is required to maintain divine favor.
4. Leadership without humility leads to rejection.
o Saul’s early humility was replaced by arrogance and self-will.
5. God’s sovereignty prevails.
o Even when human leaders fail, God’s redemptive purpose continues — as
seen in His choice of David.

Conclusion

CHAPTER 12
DAVID’S REIGN OVER ISRAEL
(1 SAMUEL 16 – 2 SAMUEL 24)
Introduction
David, the son of Jesse from the tribe of Judah, is one of the most prominent
figures in the history of Israel. His reign marked the golden age of the united
monarchy and set the foundation for the Davidic Covenant, which ultimately points
to the coming of the Messiah, Jesus Christ, from his lineage (2 Samuel 7:12–16).
David’s rise from a humble shepherd boy to the throne of Israel illustrates God’s
sovereignty, grace, and purposeful election. Although his reign was characterized by
remarkable military and spiritual achievements, it was also marred by moral
weaknesses and family crises.
The Anointing of David as King (1 Samuel 16:1–13)
A. Divine Choice and Background
• After King Saul’s disobedience and rejection, God sent Samuel to anoint a new
king from the house of Jesse in Bethlehem.
• David, the youngest son of Jesse, was chosen by God, not for outward
appearance or stature, but for the integrity of his heart.
“For the Lord sees not as man sees; man looks at the outward appearance, but the
Lord looks on the heart.” — 1 Samuel 16:7
B. The Anointing by Samuel
• Samuel anointed David with oil in the presence of his brothers (1 Samuel
16:13).
• The Spirit of the Lord came powerfully upon David from that day forward,
symbolizing divine empowerment for kingship.
• Meanwhile, the Spirit departed from Saul, marking the beginning of David’s
divine preparation and Saul’s decline (1 Samuel 16:14).
76
C. Early Service and Preparation
• David entered Saul’s service as a harpist to soothe the troubled king (1 Samuel
16:14–23).
• His victory over Goliath (1 Samuel 17) made him a national hero, revealing his
faith, courage, and dependence on God.
• His friendship with Jonathan and loyalty toward Saul even during persecution
showed his spiritual maturity and restraint.
The Establishment of David’s Kingship
A. King over Judah
• After Saul’s death, David inquired of the Lord and was anointed king over the
tribe of Judah at Hebron (2 Samuel 2:1–4).
• He reigned there for seven and a half years, while Saul’s son Ish-bosheth
ruled over the other tribes under Abner’s influence (2 Samuel 2–4).
B. King over All Israel
• After Ish-bosheth’s death, the elders of Israel came to Hebron and anointed
David as king over all Israel (2 Samuel 5:1–5).
• His reign over the united kingdom lasted 33 years, making a total of 40 years
as king (2 Samuel 5:4).
Achievements of King David
David’s reign is often described as the most prosperous period in Israel’s united
monarchy. His achievements can be grouped into political, military, religious, and
cultural dimensions.
77
A. Political Achievements
1. Unification of Israel
o David succeeded in uniting all twelve tribes under one central
government.
o This ended centuries of tribal rivalry and established national stability.
2. Establishment of Jerusalem as the Capital
o David captured the fortress of Zion from the Jebusites and made
Jerusalem the political and spiritual capital of Israel (2 Samuel 5:6–9).
o The city became known as the City of David.
3. Creation of Administrative Structures
o David organized the kingdom with officials responsible for various
administrative, military, and religious affairs (2 Samuel 8:15–18).
o He introduced a permanent civil service and military system that
sustained national governance.
B. Military Achievements
1. Defeat of Israel’s Enemies
o David subdued the Philistines, Moabites, Ammonites, Edomites, Syrians,
and Amalekites (2 Samuel 8; 10; 12).
o His victories secured Israel’s borders and expanded the kingdom’s
territory from Egypt to the Euphrates River.
2. Development of a Standing Army
o David organized a strong army led by generals like Joab and elite
warriors known as “David’s Mighty Men” (2 Samuel 23:8–39).
o The army ensured peace and stability throughout his reign.
78
C. Religious Achievements
1. Restoration of True Worship
o David brought the Ark of the Covenant to Jerusalem, symbolizing the
centralization of worship (2 Samuel 6:12–19).
o He re-established the Levitical priesthood and introduced music and
organized worship as part of Israel’s spiritual life.
2. Preparation for the Temple
o Though David was not permitted to build the Temple, he made
comprehensive preparations — gathering materials, assigning duties to
priests, and receiving divine blueprints for his son Solomon (1 Chronicles
22).
3. The Davidic Covenant
o God made an everlasting covenant with David, promising that his throne
would be established forever (2 Samuel 7:12–16).
o This covenant became the theological foundation for the expectation
of the Messiah, the Son of David.
D. Cultural and Spiritual Achievements
1. Development of Hebrew Poetry and Music
o David was a skilled poet and musician, credited with composing many of
the Psalms.
o His writings express profound worship, repentance, and devotion.
2. Promotion of Justice and Righteousness
o David’s reign was characterized by fairness and moral leadership (2
Samuel 8:15).
79
o He was a man after God’s heart (1 Samuel 13:14), known for his personal
devotion and faith.
Weaknesses and Failures of King David
Despite his deep faith and great achievements, David was not without flaws. His
moral and leadership weaknesses had lasting consequences for his family and the
nation.
A. Adultery with Bathsheba (2 Samuel 11)
• David committed adultery with Bathsheba, the wife of Uriah the Hittite, and
arranged Uriah’s death to conceal his sin.
• This act displeased the Lord, leading to divine judgment on David’s household
(2 Samuel 12:9–14).
• The prophet Nathan confronted him, leading to David’s heartfelt repentance
as seen in Psalm 51.
B. Family and Domestic Problems
• The consequences of David’s sin manifested in his family:
o Amnon’s rape of Tamar (2 Samuel 13:1–22).
o Absalom’s rebellion against his father (2 Samuel 15–18).
o Adonijah’s self-exaltation (1 Kings 1:5–10).
• These internal crises weakened David’s moral authority and brought sorrow to
his household.
C. Census and Divine Punishment (2 Samuel 24)
• Toward the end of his reign, David conducted a national census against God’s
will, showing misplaced trust in military strength rather than divine
providence.
• God punished Israel with a plague, resulting in many deaths.
80
• David repented and built an altar at Araunah’s threshing floor, the future site
of Solomon’s Temple.
D. Leniency and Leadership Weakness
• David’s excessive leniency toward his sons (especially Absalom) reflected poor
judgment in family discipline.
• His emotional leadership sometimes clouded political decisions, undermining
his stability as king.
Theological and Moral Lessons from David’s Reign
1. God values a heart of humility and repentance more than outward perfection.
2. True greatness lies in dependence on God’s grace, not personal strength.
3. Leadership involves both triumph and testing — failures can become
opportunities for restoration.
4. The Davidic Covenant points to the faithfulness of God’s promises despite
human failure.
5. Repentance restores relationship with God, as seen in David’s confession in
Psalm 51.

CHAPTER 14
THE REIGN OF KING SOLOMON
(1 KINGS 1:11–31)
Introduction
King Solomon, the son of David and Bathsheba, succeeded his father as the third
king of Israel. His reign marked the peak of Israel’s political power, economic
prosperity, and cultural development.
Solomon’s name means “peaceful”, and his reign was characterized by peace, wisdom,
wealth, and monumental building projects, most notably the construction of the
Temple in Jerusalem. However, his later years were marred by moral compromise,
idolatry, and political decline, which eventually led to the division of the kingdom
after his death.
Solomon’s Ascension to the Throne (1 Kings 1:11–31; 2:1–12)
A. Political Background
• As David’s life drew to a close, Adonijah, one of his sons, attempted to seize
the throne without divine or royal approval (1 Kings 1:5–10).
• Nathan the prophet and Bathsheba, Solomon’s mother, intervened to remind
David of his oath that Solomon would succeed him (1 Kings 1:11–14).
B. David’s Declaration
• David commanded that Solomon be anointed king immediately to prevent
Adonijah’s rebellion (1 Kings 1:32–35).
• Zadok the priest and Nathan the prophet anointed Solomon at Gihon, and the
people rejoiced, declaring, “Long live King Solomon!” (1 Kings 1:39).
• Adonijah’s rebellion collapsed as Solomon’s kingship was publicly recognized.
83
Solomon’s Early Wisdom and Obedience
• Before David’s death, he charged Solomon to:
1. Be strong and courageous, keeping God’s commandments (1 Kings 2:1–3).
2. Administer justice and maintain loyalty to God’s covenant.
• Solomon demonstrated early obedience, humility, and respect for divine
authority.
Appraisal of Solomon’s Reign and Contributions to Israel
Solomon’s reign (c. 970–930 B.C.) represented the height of Israel’s united
monarchy. His rule was marked by exceptional wisdom, wealth, peace, and prosperity.
His achievements can be grouped into political, economic, religious, and cultural
contributions.
A. Political Achievements
1. Consolidation of the Kingdom
o Solomon firmly established his authority over all Israel and subdued
internal rebellion (1 Kings 2:13–46).
o His early decisions to remove potential threats (Adonijah, Joab, and
Shimei) stabilized the monarchy.
2. Expansion of International Relations
o He maintained diplomatic relations with neighboring nations, especially
Hiram of Tyre, who supplied materials for the temple (1 Kings 5:1–12).
o Through political marriages and treaties, Solomon built alliances with
surrounding kingdoms.
3. Strong Central Government
o Solomon reorganized Israel into twelve administrative districts to
ensure efficient taxation and governance (1 Kings 4:7–19).
84
o His reign maintained peace throughout the kingdom, allowing
development and prosperity.
B. Economic Achievements
1. Commercial Expansion
o Solomon developed extensive trade networks with Tyre, Egypt, Arabia,
and Ophir (1 Kings 9:26–28; 10:22).
o He built a merchant navy at Ezion-Geber and imported gold, silver,
ivory, and precious materials.
2. National Prosperity
o Israel experienced unmatched wealth and luxury.
o “Judah and Israel were as many as the sand by the sea. They ate, drank,
and were happy.” — 1 Kings 4:20
3. Infrastructure and Building Projects
o Solomon undertook major construction works including:
▪ The Temple in Jerusalem (1 Kings 6)
▪ The Royal Palace (1 Kings 7:1–12)
▪ Fortified cities such as Hazor, Megiddo, and Gezer (1 Kings 9:15–
19)
▪ Extensive roads, storage cities, and defense systems.
C. Religious Achievements
1. Construction of the Temple
o Solomon’s greatest accomplishment was building the Temple of the Lord
in Jerusalem (1 Kings 6).
85
o It became the central place of worship and symbolized God’s presence
among His people.
o The dedication of the temple was accompanied by a moving prayer,
demonstrating Solomon’s deep reverence (1 Kings 8:22–53).
2. Organization of Worship
o Solomon arranged the priesthood and established the Levitical order
for temple services.
o The temple became a center of spiritual and national unity.
3. Promotion of Wisdom and Knowledge
o Solomon’s wisdom was legendary — he spoke 3,000 proverbs and
composed 1,005 songs (1 Kings 4:32).
o His writings are preserved in Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, and the Song of
Songs.
D. Cultural and International Influence
1. Global Reputation for Wisdom
o Solomon’s fame spread across the ancient world; rulers came to hear
his wisdom.
o The Queen of Sheba’s visit (1 Kings 10:1–10) demonstrated Israel’s
growing influence and Solomon’s international recognition.
2. Literary Contributions
o His contributions to Hebrew literature and philosophy remain
significant in biblical theology and moral instruction.
86
The Folly of King Solomon
Despite his wisdom and early devotion, Solomon’s later years were characterized by
spiritual decline and moral compromise. His actions violated the covenant principles
God had established for Israel’s kings (Deuteronomy 17:16–20).
A. Marital Alliances and Idolatry (1 Kings 11:1–8)
• Solomon loved many foreign women — Moabite, Ammonite, Edomite, Sidonian,
and Hittite — marrying about 700 wives and 300 concubines.
• These alliances introduced idolatry into Israel.
• He built high places for the gods Chemosh and Molech, provoking the Lord’s
anger.
• His heart turned away from full devotion to the Lord his God.
B. Materialism and Pride
• Solomon’s accumulation of wealth, luxury, and horses violated divine commands
(Deuteronomy 17:16–17).
• His dependence on material success and human alliances replaced trust in God.
• His love for grandeur led to heavy taxation and forced labor, breeding
resentment among the people (1 Kings 12:4).
C. Oppression and Administrative Corruption
• Solomon’s building projects relied heavily on compulsory labor, causing
hardship among the Israelites (1 Kings 5:13–14).
• The people’s complaints about his harsh rule later ignited rebellion under
Jeroboam after his death.
D. Divine Judgment
• Because of Solomon’s idolatry, God declared that the kingdom would be
divided after his death (1 Kings 11:9–13).
87
• Though God spared Solomon during his lifetime for David’s sake, the unity of
Israel would not continue.
Theological and Moral Lessons from Solomon’s Reign
1. Wisdom without obedience leads to destruction.
o Knowledge and success cannot replace faithfulness to God’s word.
2. Wealth and power are tests of integrity.
o Prosperity can become a snare if not governed by humility.
3. Disobedience in leadership affects generations.
o Solomon’s sins led to the eventual division of the kingdom.
4. God’s covenant faithfulness endures human failure.
o Despite Solomon’s downfall, God preserved the Davidic line for the
coming Messiah.
5. True greatness lies in fearing God.
o As Solomon himself concluded:
“Fear God and keep His commandments, for this is the whole duty of man.” —
Ecclesiastes 12:13

division.
His life teaches that wisdom without devotion is hollow and that true success lies in
constant obedience to God.
Ultimately, Solomon’s story points forward to Jesus Christ, the greater Son of

CHAPTER 15
THE DIVIDED KINGDOM AND THE EXILES
(1 Kings 12:1–25)
Introduction
The division of the United Kingdom of Israel marked a turning point in the nation’s
history. After the glorious reigns of David and Solomon, Israel split into two rival
kingdoms — the Northern Kingdom (Israel) with its capital at Samaria, and the
Southern Kingdom (Judah) with its capital at Jerusalem.
This tragic event was the direct result of spiritual decline, political mismanagement,
and divine judgment. The division ultimately led to the weakening of both kingdoms
and their eventual exile — Israel to Assyria (722 B.C.), and Judah to Babylon (586
B.C.).
Factors that Led to the Division of the Kingdom (1 Kings 12:1–25)
The division did not happen suddenly; it was the outcome of several long-term and
immediate causes — political, economic, social, and spiritual.
A. Solomon’s Apostasy and Oppression
1. Religious Apostasy
o Solomon’s later years were marked by idolatry due to his many foreign
wives (1 Kings 11:1–8).
o His disobedience provoked divine anger, and God foretold that the
kingdom would be divided after his death (1 Kings 11:9–13).
2. Economic Exploitation
o Heavy taxation and forced labor to fund Solomon’s grand projects
created deep resentment among the northern tribes (1 Kings 12:4).
o The people felt oppressed by the extravagant policies of the monarchy.
90
B. Tribal Discontent and Political Rivalry
• The northern tribes felt marginalized, as the royal court, temple, and major
institutions were all centered in Jerusalem (Judah).
• Ephraim, a powerful northern tribe, resented Judah’s dominance since David’s
reign.
• Regional tensions created a strong sense of tribal rivalry and separatism.
C. The Rebellion of Jeroboam
• Jeroboam, one of Solomon’s trusted officials, rebelled against him after being
told by the prophet Ahijah the Shilonite that he would rule ten tribes of
Israel (1 Kings 11:26–39).
• Although Jeroboam fled to Egypt for safety, his return after Solomon’s death
reignited the northern tribes’ hope for independence.
D. Rehoboam’s Harsh Policy
• When Solomon died, Rehoboam, his son, went to Shechem to be made king (1
Kings 12:1).
• The northern tribes requested relief from the heavy burdens imposed by
Solomon.
• Rehoboam rejected the counsel of the elders and listened to the advice of his
young peers, saying:
“My father made your yoke heavy, but I will add to your yoke.” — 1 Kings 12:14
• This arrogance alienated the people and led to revolt.
E. Divine Judgment
• The division fulfilled God’s prophetic word through Ahijah, who told Solomon
that the kingdom would be torn apart because of his sins (1 Kings 11:29–39).
• Thus, the division was not merely political but theological — a divine act of
judgment for covenant unfaithfulness.
91
Summary of Causes:
• Spiritual decline and idolatry
• Economic exploitation and forced labor
• Tribal rivalry and jealousy
• Political insensitivity by Rehoboam
• Divine retribution for covenant disobedience
The Reign and Activities of Rehoboam of Judah (1 Kings 12:1–24;
14:21–31)
A. Accession and Early Mistakes
• Rehoboam, son of Solomon, succeeded his father as king over Judah.
• His unwise decision to ignore wise counsel triggered the secession of the ten
northern tribes, leaving him with only Judah and Benjamin.
• His attempt to suppress the revolt through Adoram, the tax collector, failed
— Adoram was stoned to death (1 Kings 12:18).
B. Political and Religious Condition of His Reign
1. Establishment of the Southern Kingdom (Judah)
o Rehoboam ruled from Jerusalem and tried to fortify cities for defense
(2 Chronicles 11:5–12).
o His reign lasted seventeen years (c. 931–913 B.C.).
2. Religious Backsliding
o For the first three years, Judah was faithful to God (2 Chronicles
11:17).
o Later, Rehoboam and Judah forsook the law of the Lord and built high
places, pillars, and Asherim (1 Kings 14:22–24).
92
3. Egyptian Invasion
o As a form of divine punishment, Shishak, king of Egypt, invaded
Jerusalem, looted the temple and palace treasures, and carried away
the golden shields Solomon had made (1 Kings 14:25–26).
o Rehoboam replaced them with bronze shields — a symbol of decline
from former glory.

CHAPTER 16
THE EXILES: AN OVERVIEW
(2 Kings 16–25)
Introduction
The Exile represents one of the most significant and tragic periods in the history of
Israel. It marks the end of the united monarchy and national independence, and the
beginning of a time of foreign domination and spiritual reformation.
The term “Exile” refers to the forced deportation of the Israelites from their
homeland to foreign nations — first by the Assyrians (Israel, 722 B.C.), and later by
the Babylonians (Judah, 586 B.C.).
This catastrophic period fulfilled God’s warnings through the prophets that
disobedience and idolatry would lead to national judgment. Despite the tragedy, the
exile also became a period of spiritual renewal and redefinition of Israel’s faith.
Historical Background to the Exiles
• The story of the exiles is found mainly in 2 Kings 16–25 and 2 Chronicles 28–
36.
• It covers the final years of both kingdoms:
o The Northern Kingdom (Israel) fell to Assyria in 722 B.C. during the
reign of Hoshea.
o The Southern Kingdom (Judah) fell to Babylon in 586 B.C. during the
reign of Zedekiah.
• The Assyrian and Babylonian conquests were not just political events — they
were theological judgments for covenant unfaithfulness (Deuteronomy 28:15–
68).
97
Causes of the Exiles (2 Kings 17:1–23; 24:1–20)
The exile was not a sudden disaster but the result of centuries of persistent
disobedience to God’s law. The prophets had long warned that idolatry, injustice, and
rebellion would bring divine punishment.
A. Spiritual and Religious Causes
1. Idolatry and Syncretism
o The people of Israel abandoned the worship of Yahweh and served
other gods (2 Kings 17:7–12).
o They set up Asherah poles, Baal shrines, and high places throughout the
land.
o The kings tolerated and encouraged false worship, especially
Jeroboam’s golden calves (2 Kings 17:16).
2. Rejection of Prophetic Warnings
o God sent prophets like Elijah, Elisha, Isaiah, Hosea, Jeremiah, and
others to call the people to repentance.
o The people mocked the messengers of God, hardened their hearts, and
continued in sin (2 Kings 17:13–14).
3. Violation of the Covenant
o Israel broke the Sinaitic Covenant by ignoring God’s commands and
embracing pagan customs (2 Kings 17:15).
o Covenant disloyalty led to divine wrath and national expulsion.
B. Moral and Social Corruption
1. Injustice and Oppression
o The rich oppressed the poor, and leaders perverted justice (Amos 2:6–
8; Micah 3:9–11).
98
o Society was marked by moral decay, violence, and neglect of the needy.
2. Corruption in Leadership
o Kings, priests, and prophets all contributed to the nation’s spiritual
collapse.
o Instead of guiding the people to God, they led them astray through
falsehood and greed.
C. Political Causes
1. Foreign Alliances
o Israel and Judah sought help from pagan nations (Egypt, Assyria,
Babylon) instead of trusting God (2 Kings 16:7–9).
o These alliances weakened their independence and invited foreign
interference.
2. Rebellion Against Imperial Powers
o King Hoshea of Israel rebelled against Assyria, leading to the Assyrian
invasion (2 Kings 17:1–6).
o Similarly, Jehoiakim, Jehoiachin, and Zedekiah of Judah rebelled
against Babylon, resulting in successive deportations (2 Kings 24:1–20).
D. Divine Judgment
• Ultimately, the exile was the execution of God’s judgment as foretold in the
Law (Deuteronomy 28:36–64).
• God’s patience had limits; continued rebellion brought His wrath.
• The exile served as a disciplinary measure to purify and restore His people.
99
Summary of Causes:
• Idolatry and false worship
• Rejection of God’s prophets
• Social injustice and moral decay
• Political rebellion and foreign dependence
• Fulfillment of divine judgment
The Effects of the Exiles
The exile had far-reaching spiritual, social, and political effects on Israel and
Judah. It reshaped the identity and faith of the Hebrew people forever.
A. Political and National Effects
1. Loss of Independence
o Israel ceased to exist as a sovereign nation.
o Judah was reduced to a vassal state and later destroyed by Babylon.
2. Destruction of Jerusalem and the Temple
o The Babylonians burned the city, broke down its walls, and destroyed
Solomon’s Temple (2 Kings 25:9–10).
o This ended the central system of worship in Jerusalem.
3. Deportation of the Population
o Thousands of Israelites were carried away to Assyria (2 Kings 17:6).
o Later, the Babylonians deported the Jews in three stages (605, 597,
and 586 B.C.), leaving only the poor behind (2 Kings 24–25).
100
B. Social and Economic Effects
1. Displacement and Hardship
o Families were separated, and land ownership was lost.
o The exiles became servants and strangers in foreign lands.
2. Decline of Agricultural Life
o Fields and vineyards lay desolate.
o The once prosperous land of Canaan became impoverished.
3. Cultural Assimilation
o Many Israelites adopted foreign languages, names, and customs, losing
touch with their heritage.
C. Religious and Spiritual Effects
1. Loss of the Temple Worship
o Without the temple, sacrifices could no longer be offered.
o The people developed new forms of worship — synagogue gatherings,
prayer, and Torah study.
2. Renewal of Faith
o The exile became a turning point of repentance and spiritual reflection
(cf. Daniel 9:1–19).
o The people realized that true worship was not limited to a temple but
centered on obedience to God’s word.
3. Emergence of the Written Scriptures
o Prophets and scribes began to compile, preserve, and teach the sacred
writings, ensuring that the Law and Prophets were not forgotten.
o This period saw the growth of Judaism as a religion of the Book.
101
4. Hope of Restoration
o Prophets like Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and Isaiah offered hope of return and
renewal.
o The idea of a new covenant and a Messianic hope took root during this
time (Jeremiah 31:31–34; Ezekiel 36:24–28).
The State of the Exiles (2 Kings 25)
A. Conditions in Judah after the Fall
1. Destruction and Desolation
o Jerusalem and its temple were completely destroyed (2 Kings 25:9–10).
o Only the poorest people were left to work the land (2 Kings 25:12).
2. Governorship of Gedaliah
o The Babylonians appointed Gedaliah, son of Ahikam, as governor over
the remnant in Judah (2 Kings 25:22).
o He encouraged the people to serve Babylon peacefully, but he was
assassinated by Ishmael, a royal descendant (2 Kings 25:25).
3. Flight to Egypt
o Following Gedaliah’s death, many survivors fled to Egypt, fearing
Babylonian retaliation (2 Kings 25:26).
o This act of fear and disobedience further demonstrated their lack of
trust in God.
B. Life in Exile
1. Captivity in Babylon
o The exiles lived as captives but were allowed to settle, farm, and engage
in trade.
102
o Some, like Daniel and Ezekiel, gained influence in Babylonian society
while maintaining faith in God.
2. Religious Transformation
o The exiles emphasized personal piety, prayer, and the study of the Law.
o The synagogue system emerged as a center of worship and teaching.
3. Hope for Return
o Prophets continually reminded the people that exile was temporary and
that God would restore them after seventy years (Jeremiah 29:10).
o This hope sustained their identity and faith amid hardship.
Theological and Moral Lessons from the Exile
1. Sin Brings Judgment
o Persistent disobedience leads to divine punishment, both personally and
nationally.
2. God’s Covenant is Just and Faithful
o God kept His word — both in judgment and in mercy.
3. Repentance Leads to Restoration
o The exile taught Israel that repentance opens the way for renewal and
blessing.
4. True Worship is Spiritual, not Merely Ritual
o The absence of the temple revealed that God desires obedience and
purity of heart over sacrifices.
5. Hope in God’s Promises
o Even in exile, God preserved His people and promised redemption —
ultimately fulfilled through the coming of Christ.

CHAPTER 17
THE RISE OF PROPHECY IN ISRAEL
(Early Manifestation of Prophecy in Israel)
Introduction
Prophecy played a central role in the spiritual and national life of Israel. It was
through the prophets that God revealed His will, warned against sin, guided His
people, and unfolded His redemptive purpose.
The rise of prophecy was not an accident but a divine initiative—a response to the
moral, political, and spiritual needs of the nation. From the earliest leaders like
Moses and Joshua to the later prophets such as Samuel and Elijah, prophecy evolved
into a powerful institution that shaped Israel’s destiny.
Meaning of Prophecy
The word “prophecy” comes from the Hebrew term nabi, meaning “one who is called”
or “one who speaks for another.”
In the Old Testament context, a prophet was a person called and inspired by God to
deliver His message to individuals or the nation.
A prophet, therefore, is not primarily a predictor of the future but a forth-teller
of God’s will, speaking truth to power and calling people back to covenant
faithfulness.
The Rise of Pre-Canonical Prophets in Israel
Before the establishment of the prophetic writings (Isaiah–Malachi), there were
early prophets, known as pre-canonical prophets, whose ministries laid the
foundation for Israel’s prophetic tradition.
A. Moses – The Foundational Prophet (Deuteronomy 18:15–18)
• Moses is regarded as the first and greatest prophet of Israel.
105
• He was called by God at the burning bush (Exodus 3:1–10) and became the
mediator of the covenant between God and Israel.
• Through him, God revealed the Law (Torah) and led Israel out of Egypt.
• Moses’ prophetic ministry combined legislation, leadership, and intercession.
B. Joshua – The Successor Prophet-Leader
• Joshua succeeded Moses and continued the divine mission of leading Israel
into the Promised Land (Joshua 1:1–9).
• His prophetic role was military and administrative, ensuring obedience to God’s
covenant.
• Joshua demonstrated that prophecy was not only about words but also about
faithful action and spiritual leadership.
C. Samuel – The Transitional Prophet (1 Samuel 3:1–20)
• Samuel marks the transition from the period of the Judges to the monarchical
period.
• His calling as a young boy in the temple showed the revival of prophetic
revelation at a time when “the word of the Lord was rare” (1 Samuel 3:1).
• He served as prophet, priest, and judge, anointing both Saul and David as
kings.
• Samuel also established the School of the Prophets, a prophetic institution
for training young men in divine service.
D. Nathan, Elijah, and Elisha – Prophets in the Monarchical Period
1. Nathan (2 Samuel 12:1–15)
o Served during David’s reign.
o Rebuked King David for his sin with Bathsheba, demonstrating the
prophet’s duty to speak truth to authority.
106
2. Elijah (1 Kings 17–19)
o A fearless prophet who confronted Ahab and Jezebel over Baal
worship.
o Performed miracles and defended monotheism at Mount Carmel.
o His life symbolized zeal, courage, and divine power.
3. Elisha (2 Kings 2–9)
o Successor of Elijah.
o His ministry focused on miracles of healing, restoration, and provision.
o He ministered compassionately, showing that prophecy also involved
mercy and social concern.
E. Prophetic Guilds and the School of the Prophets
• Under Samuel, organized groups of prophets known as “sons of the prophets”
emerged (1 Samuel 10:5; 19:20).
• These communities promoted spiritual education, worship, and moral reform.
• They preserved prophetic traditions and provided mentorship for younger
prophets.
The School of the Prophets emphasized:
• Devotion to God
• Prayer and meditation
• Scriptural study and teaching
• Prophetic training and discipline
107
Characteristics of Prophecy in Israel
The ministry of prophecy in Israel possessed distinctive spiritual and moral features
that set it apart from other ancient Near Eastern prophetic traditions.
A. Divine Inspiration
• True prophecy originated from the Spirit of God, not human imagination (2
Peter 1:21).
• Prophets received divine messages through visions, dreams, audible voices, or
inner revelation.
• Inspiration ensured that the prophet’s message reflected God’s authority, not
personal opinion.
B. Call to Repentance
• Prophets were moral reformers.
• Their primary message was “Return to the Lord” — calling the people away
from sin, idolatry, and injustice.
• They urged national repentance and obedience to the covenant (cf. Hosea 14:1–
2).
C. Dedication and Holiness
• Prophets lived lives of personal consecration and devotion to God.
• They were examples of piety, often living in solitude or separation from
corrupt society.
• Their lives demonstrated that the messenger’s character must reflect the
holiness of the message.
D. Fearlessness and Moral Courage
• Prophets spoke boldly against kings, priests, and nations regardless of the
consequences.
108
• Their loyalty to God outweighed fear of men (cf. 1 Kings 18:17–18).
• Elijah’s confrontation with Ahab and Nathan’s rebuke of David exemplify
prophetic fearlessness.
E. Faithfulness to the Word of God
• Prophets maintained absolute fidelity to God’s revelation.
• False prophets often spoke what people wanted to hear, but true prophets
declared only what God commanded (Jeremiah 23:16–22).
F. Compassion and Intercession
• Prophets often served as intercessors, pleading with God for mercy on behalf
of the people (Exodus 32:11–14; Jeremiah 14:7–9).
• They reflected God’s heart of love, justice, and redemption.
The Role of the Prophet in Israel
The prophet was much more than a foreteller of events; he was a spiritual leader,
reformer, and national conscience.
A. Messenger of God
• The prophet served as God’s spokesperson, delivering messages of warning,
guidance, encouragement, and judgment.
• His authority came from divine commissioning, not political appointment.
Formula often used: “Thus says the Lord…” (Isaiah 1:2; Jeremiah 2:2).
B. Defender of True Worship
• Prophets maintained the purity of Yahweh worship against idolatry and
syncretism.
• They called the nation back to exclusive devotion to God (Deuteronomy 6:4–
5).
109
• Through their ministry, they preserved ethical monotheism—the worship of
one holy God with moral obedience.
C. Guardian of the Covenant
• Prophets reminded Israel of their covenant responsibilities and the
consequences of disobedience.
• They interpreted historical events (war, drought, exile) as divine responses
to moral and spiritual conditions.
D. Advisor to Kings and Leaders
• Many prophets served as royal counselors, guiding the nation in crises.
• Examples include:
o Samuel advising Saul and David
o Nathan correcting David
o Isaiah counseling Hezekiah (2 Kings 19:1–7)
The prophet’s loyalty was to God’s authority, not to political power.
E. Founder and Teacher in the School of the Prophets
• Prophets like Samuel organized schools where younger men were trained in
spiritual disciplines and the word of God.
• These institutions preserved moral order, promoted education, and served as
centers of spiritual revival.
F. Encourager of Pious Worship and Social Justice
• Prophets condemned empty rituals and emphasized justice, mercy, and
humility before God (Micah 6:6–8).
• True worship, they taught, was shown through ethical living and compassion
toward others.
110
6. Theological and Moral Lessons from the Rise of Prophecy
1. God Always Raises a Voice for Truth.
o In every generation, God calls men and women to speak His word
faithfully.
2. Divine Revelation Demands Obedience.
o Hearing God’s word is not enough; obedience is the true response to
prophecy.
3. Moral Integrity is Essential for Spiritual Authority.
o Prophets were powerful because they were pure in heart and purpose.
4. Prophetic Courage Inspires Reform.
o The courage of prophets like Elijah and Nathan teaches believers to
confront evil boldly.
5. True Worship Involves Justice and Holiness.
o Prophets remind us that religion without righteousness is hypocrisy.

The rise of prophecy in Israel marks a divine intervention in human history.

CHAPTER 18
THE MAJOR AND MINOR PROPHETS
(THEMES FROM ISAIAH, HOSEA, AND AMOS)
Introduction
The prophets of Israel were God’s spokesmen, raised to guide, rebuke, comfort, and
restore His people. Their messages were not only for their own generation but also
for the moral and spiritual development of future societies.
They addressed issues of holiness, justice, love, idolatry, oppression, and national
decay, thereby laying down timeless principles for nation building.
Distinction Between the Major and Minor Prophets
The distinction between the Major and Minor prophets is based not on importance,
but on the length and scope of their writings.

Prophet Isaiah and His Message on Holiness (Isaiah 1–3)
A. Background
• Isaiah, son of Amoz, prophesied during the reigns of Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz,
and Hezekiah (Isaiah 1:1).
• His ministry occurred in the 8th century B.C., a time of moral decay, social
injustice, and political instability in Judah.
• His prophetic call (Isaiah 6:1–8) emphasized the holiness of God — a central
theme of his entire message.
“Holy, holy, holy is the LORD of hosts; the whole earth is full of His glory.” — Isaiah
6:3
B. Isaiah’s Message on Holiness
1. God’s Absolute Holiness:
o Isaiah presents God as pure, righteous, and perfect.
o Sin separates man from this holy God (Isaiah 59:1–2).
2. Call to Repentance and Moral Cleansing:
o The prophet condemns empty religious rituals and calls for moral purity
and social justice (Isaiah 1:11–17).
o “Wash yourselves, make yourselves clean… cease to do evil, learn to do
good.”
3. Judgment and Restoration:
o Isaiah warns that judgment will come upon Judah because of hypocrisy
and rebellion.
o Yet, he also promises restoration through a purified remnant (Isaiah
1:18–27).
114
4. The Holy God and the Holy People:
o God’s purpose is to create a nation that reflects His holiness in conduct,
worship, and governance.
C. Relevance of Isaiah’s Message on Holiness to Nation Building
1. Moral Foundation for Leadership:
o No nation can stand without moral integrity. Isaiah’s call to holiness
emphasizes righteous governance and ethical leadership.
2. Purity in Public Life:
o True national progress depends on clean hands and pure hearts (Psalm
24:3–4).
3. Holiness as Social Responsibility:
o Holiness extends beyond personal piety to include justice, compassion,
and honesty in societal relationships.
4. National Renewal through Repentance:
o National revival begins with a return to God and a rejection of
corruption and moral decay.
Lesson: A holy people will produce a stable, just, and prosperous nation.
Prophet Hosea and His Message on Love (Hosea 1–3)
A. Background
• Hosea prophesied to the Northern Kingdom (Israel) during a time of spiritual
adultery and moral decay.
• God commanded Hosea to marry Gomer, an unfaithful woman, as a symbolic act
to represent God’s relationship with unfaithful Israel.
115
• His message centered on God’s steadfast love (hesed) and Israel’s
unfaithfulness.
B. Hosea’s Message on Divine Love
1. Symbolic Marriage (Hosea 1–2):
o Hosea’s marriage demonstrates God’s covenant love for His people
despite their repeated betrayals.
o Israel’s idolatry is likened to spiritual adultery, but God still yearns for
her return.
2. God’s Unfailing Love and Mercy (Hosea 3):
o Hosea redeems Gomer, symbolizing God’s forgiveness and restoration
of Israel.
o Divine love is patient, redemptive, and sacrificial.
3. Call to Repentance:
o Hosea urges Israel to return to God and renounce false gods (Hosea
6:1–3).
o Love must be reflected in faithfulness and obedience.
C. Implications of Hosea’s Message for Israel
1. Covenant Loyalty:
o God’s love demands loyalty and faithfulness from His people.
2. Restoration through Repentance:
o Even after judgment, love opens the door for reconciliation.
3. Love as the Basis of Relationship:
o True religion is not ritual but a relationship built on love and devotion
(Hosea 6:6).
116
“For I desire mercy, not sacrifice, and acknowledgment of God rather than burnt
offerings.” — Hosea 6:6
D. Relevance of Hosea’s Message on Love to Nation Building
1. Promotes Unity and Forgiveness:
o A nation built on love overcomes hatred, ethnic rivalry, and division.
2. Encourages Compassionate Leadership:
o Leaders who govern with love and empathy build trust and national
cohesion.
3. Restores Broken Relationships:
o Love leads to reconciliation among citizens, healing the wounds of
corruption, injustice, and social inequality.
4. Reflects God’s Character in Society:
o A loving nation becomes a moral light to others.
Lesson: Love is the glue that holds a nation together and transforms broken
societies into compassionate communities.
Prophet Amos and His Message on Justice (Amos 1–5)
A. Background
• Amos, a shepherd and dresser of sycamore trees from Tekoa, was called to
prophesy to the Northern Kingdom during the reign of Jeroboam II (8th
century B.C.).
• His era was marked by economic prosperity, but also moral corruption,
oppression, and social injustice.
• Amos’ message focused on divine justice, righteousness, and the responsibility
of the rich and powerful to uphold fairness.
117
B. Amos’ Message on Justice
1. Condemnation of Social Injustice:
o The prophet denounces the oppression of the poor, bribery, and corrupt
leadership (Amos 2:6–8).
o “They sell the righteous for silver, and the needy for a pair of sandals.”
2. The Call for Righteousness:
o Amos emphasizes that justice and righteousness are the true marks of
God’s people (Amos 5:24).
o “Let justice roll down like waters, and righteousness like a mighty
stream.”
3. Rejection of Hypocritical Religion:
o God despises empty religious ceremonies that lack moral integrity
(Amos 5:21–23).
4. Judgment for Injustice:
o God’s wrath will fall upon those who exploit others and ignore His moral
standards.
C. Relevance of Amos’ Message on Justice to Nation Building
1. Foundation for Good Governance:
o Justice ensures peace, stability, and confidence in national institutions.
2. Social Equity:
o A just society uplifts the poor and defends the oppressed.
3. Moral Integrity in Leadership:
o Justice demands that leaders act with honesty, fairness, and
accountability.
118
4. Sustainable Peace:
o Peace is impossible without justice; inequality breeds rebellion and
violence.
Lesson: Justice is the lifeblood of any nation that desires to thrive under God’s
blessing.
The Relevance of Prophetic Themes to Nation Building
The messages of Isaiah, Hosea, and Amos provide timeless blueprints for moral and
national transformation. Each theme—holiness, love, and justice—addresses a
foundational need for every nation.

Conclusion:
• Holiness purifies the heart of the nation.
• Love binds the people in unity.
• Justice sustains peace and prosperity.
Together, they form a triad of divine principles that can transform any society into
a community of righteousness and peace.
Summary and Reflection
1. Isaiah teaches that holiness is the foundation of true nationhood.
2. Hosea reveals that love is the force that heals and reconciles.
3. Amos reminds us that justice is the structure that preserves righteousness.
4. For a nation to prosper, its citizens and leaders must reflect God’s character
in holiness, love, and justice.
“Righteousness exalts a nation, but sin is a reproach to any people.” — Proverbs
14:34
